\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textsc{KakeyaLattice}, a KV-cache compression codec
for transformer LLMs that constructs a \emph{discrete Kakeya
cover} over the direction sphere via a Zamir--Feder nested-lattice
quantiser~\cite{zamir-feder}. The paper covers two concrete
instantiations of a single codec family: a $D_4$ nested lattice
variant and an $E_8$ nested lattice variant, sharing the same
nine-step pipeline (unit-norm factorisation, Sylvester--Hadamard
rotation, per-vector adaptive $q_\mathrm{max}$, joint scale,
lattice closest-point, clamp). The key design innovation is
adaptation to the measured non-Gaussian structure of real LLM KV
activations (sub-Gaussian body, per-coordinate heavy tail after
rotation, coordinate anisotropy up to $4.71\times$ on Qwen3-4B
post-QK-norm $K$); without these levers the predicted shaping
gain does not manifest. The lattice Voronoi cells replace the
cube cells of $\Z^N$, trading $G(\Z^N)=1/12$ for
$G(D_4)\approx 0.0766$~\cite{conway-sloane-book} or
$G(E_8)\approx 0.0717$.

Measured results are live-vLLM on NVIDIA H200 under two protocols.
Under \emph{snapshot} evaluation the $D_4$ variant wins $12/12$
on K-MSE ($10$--$36\%$ better) and $9/12$ on $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$
across four open-source models at three near-matched bit tiers;
the theoretical $G(D_4)/G(\Z^4)\approx 0.919$ shaping ratio is
recovered to within $\sim\!1\%$ in three independent environments.
Under \emph{in-forward rigorous} evaluation ($n=32$, $95\%$ CI,
no-boundary) the $E_8$ variant reduces
$|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ by $28$--$53\%$ across three deployable
models at $Q\in\{4,10\}$, with $+1.3$ to $+2.0\,$dB per-layer
K-MSE gain over $D_4$ --- $4$--$6\times$ the $+0.29\,$dB
theoretical minimum, because $E_8$'s two-coset structure handles
coarse-quantisation outliers better than $D_4$'s single parity
flip. Cost: $+16$--$32$ packed bits/vector ($-5$ to $-7\%$ CR at
iso-$q_\mathrm{range}$) and $1.56$--$1.80\times$ encode latency.
Long-context retrieval (Needle-in-a-Haystack at $16$k) is
preserved on Qwen3-4B and Gemma-4-E4B.
Strict-GPU, no mock / simplification / fallback / overfit;
bit-level regression gated by a pinned \texttt{sha256}
frozen-parity test ($8$ hashes). Code, per-passage JSON, four
per-architecture attention hooks, and the multi-model / NIAH /
latency harnesses are released under Apache-2.0.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The key--value (KV) cache is the dominant memory constraint in
production LLM inference~\cite{h2o,scissorhands,deepseekv3}. At
context length $T$ with $L$ layers, $H_{kv}$ heads, head dim $d$,
the bf16 KV footprint is $4LH_{kv}dT$ bytes --- $18$\,GiB per user
for Qwen3-4B at $T=128{,}000$, several hundred GiB each for the
April-2026 flagship open-source models (DeepSeek-V3.1, Kimi-K2,
Qwen3-235B, GLM-4.6). Compressing this object post-hoc (without
re-training) is one of the highest-leverage algorithmic targets in
current serving infrastructure.

\paragraph{Lattice shaping, unexploited.}
Every production KV codec we know of quantises the
Hadamard-rotated residual \emph{per coordinate}, implicitly using
the cube cell of $\Z^N$ whose normalised second moment is
$G(\Z^N)=1/12$~\cite{zamir-feder,conway-sloane-book}. Shannon's
bound for lattice $\Lambda$ is
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:shaping-gap}
  \text{MSE}_\Lambda = G(\Lambda)\cdot 2^{-2b}, \quad G(\Lambda)\leq G(\Z^N),
\end{equation}
so using $\Lambda \in \{A_2, D_4, E_8, \Lambda_{24}\}$ with
$G(\Lambda)\in\{0.0802, 0.0766, 0.0717, 0.0658\}$ yields
$0.17/0.37/0.65/1.03\,$dB of unconditional \emph{shaping gain}
over $\Z^N$ at matched rate. This is free
MSE reduction, constrained only by lattice encode cost.

\paragraph{The codec as a discrete Kakeya cover.}
\label{sec:intro-kakeya}
The classical Kakeya
problem~\cite{kakeya-problem,besicovitch-1919,wolff-kakeya,tao-kakeya-survey,dvir-finite-field,wang-zahl}
asks for the minimum measure of a set in $\R^D$ containing a unit
segment in every direction. A KV-cache codec reconstructing each
$x_i$ to $\ell_2$-error $\varepsilon$ must cover a tube of radius
$\varepsilon$ around each direction in
$\Theta=\{x_i/\norm{x_i}\}\subset S^{D-1}$ --- exactly what the
Kakeya maximal-function conjecture bounds.
\textsc{KakeyaLattice}'s codebook realises the discrete version of
this cover: the tensor-product $D_4^{\otimes D/4}$ (or
$E_8^{\otimes D/8}$) Voronoi cells, scaled adaptively per vector
by $q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$, tile $\R^D$ so that every
direction falls within one cell. The cover's second-moment
optimum is unconditional at the codec's block dimension,
replacing the conditional Kakeya lower-bound used in our prior
low-rank codec~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper}.

\subsection{Contributions}

(i) A discrete Kakeya cover with working $O(D)$ GPU decoder
(Conway--Sloane closest-point + one parity
flip~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}). (ii) Non-Gaussian adaptation as the
main engineering lever: five pre-processing levers
(\S\ref{sec:codec-why}) against the specific mismatches measured
in Qwen3-4B $K$ ($|\text{excess kurt}|=0.84$, RMS
Wasserstein-$2/\sigma=0.65$ per dim, variance ratio $4.71\times$),
all past their Gaussian-check gates. (iii) An unconditional
rate--distortion boundary at the codec's block dimension via $D_4$
/ $E_8$ second-moment optimality. (iv) Architecture-independent
gain across four heterogeneous transformer families (Qwen3-4B,
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-1.5B, Gemma-4-E4B at
$\text{head\_dim}=256$, GLM-4-9B-Chat): K-MSE ratio within
$\sim\!1\%$ of $0.919$ on every model. (v) Strict-GPU live-vLLM
evaluation under two protocols chosen per question: snapshot
(\S\ref{sec:methodology-snapshot}; $n=4$) for the $D_4$ headline
in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks}; in-forward rigorous
(\S\ref{sec:methodology-rigorous}; $n=32$, $95\%$ CI,
no-boundary) for the $E_8$-vs-$D_4$ comparison in \S\ref{sec:v15};
every results-table caption cites its protocol. (vi) $128$k KV
storage reduction of $2.5$--$2.8\times$ balanced /
$1.66$--$1.73\times$ near-lossless with both $K$ and $V$
compressed ($|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|<1\%$, top-1 $\geq 98\%$ at the
near-lossless point).

\section{Design philosophy: the Kakeya--Brascamp--Lieb--Tropp chain}
\label{sec:framework}

Let $X = \{x_i\}_{i=1}^n \subset \R^D$ be a block of $n$ cached $K$
vectors at head dimension $D$ (post-QK-norm pre-RoPE), inducing a
direction set $\Theta = \{x_i/\norm{x_i}\} \subset S^{D-1}$. We
take squared-$\ell_2$ distortion
$\rho(x,\hat x)=\norm{x-\hat x}_2^2$ (see \S\ref{sec:mse-discussion}
on the MSE vs.\ inner-product choice). A codec reconstructing every
$x_i$ to $\ell_2$-error $\varepsilon$ must cover a tube of radius
$\varepsilon$ around every direction in $\Theta$; the minimum cost
of such a tube-union is exactly what the classical Kakeya
maximal-function conjecture
bounds~\cite{kakeya-problem,besicovitch-1919,wolff-kakeya,tao-kakeya-survey}.
\textsc{KakeyaLattice} realises the discrete version of this cover
explicitly via the $D_4$ (or $E_8$) Voronoi tessellation.

\begin{proposition}[Kakeya-conditional rate--distortion lower bound, informal]
\label{prop:kakeya-rd}
Let $\Theta \subset S^{D-1}$ have metric-entropy dimension
$d_\delta(\Theta)$. Conditional on the Kakeya maximal-function
conjecture in dimension $D$, the minimum byte budget for
$\ell_2$-error $\varepsilon\norm{x_i}$ satisfies
$B(\varepsilon,n,D) = \Omega(n\log_2\tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}
+ d_\delta(\Theta)\log_2\tfrac{1}{\varepsilon})$. At $D=3$ the bound
is unconditional~\cite{wang-zahl}; at $D\geq 4$ it remains
conditional.
\end{proposition}

The bound sits at the deep end of a published four-link chain:
\emph{multilinear Kakeya endpoint}~\cite{guth-endpoint}
$\to$ \emph{Brascamp--Lieb
equivalence}~\cite{bennett-carbery-christ-tao} $\to$ \emph{matrix
concentration} (Tropp's matrix Chernoff via Lieb
concavity~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff,carbery-valdimarsson}) $\to$
\emph{lattice second-moment
optimality}~\cite{zamir-feder,conway-sloane-book}. The codec uses
the chain's shallow end (link 4), which is \emph{unconditional} at
the codec's block dimension:

\begin{proposition}[$D_4$ Voronoi cells as a restricted Kakeya cover]
\label{prop:d4-kakeya}
After unit-norm factorisation and Sylvester--Hadamard rotation, let
$\Lambda = D_4^{\otimes K}$ with $K = D/4$ scaled by
$q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$. Its Voronoi cells
direction-cover $S^{D-1}$ with covering radius
$\varrho(\Lambda)^2 \leq q_\mathrm{max}^2 \cdot G(D_4)
/ [q_\mathrm{range}^2 \cdot (2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^{4K/D}]$, where
$G(D_4)\approx 0.0766 < G(\Z^4) = 1/12 \approx 0.0833$. The ratio
$G(D_4)/G(\Z^4) \approx 0.919$ is the MSE shaping gain
($+0.367\,$dB). The same statement holds for $\Lambda = E_8^{\otimes K}$
at $K = D/8$ with $G(E_8)\approx 0.0717$ ($+0.65\,$dB over
$\Z^8$, or $+0.29\,$dB over $D_4$). \qed
\end{proposition}

\noindent The proof is immediate from the second-moment formula for
Voronoi cells~\cite{conway-sloane-book} and the Gaussian
channel-coding
view~\cite{zamir-feder,eyuboglu-forney,erez-zamir}; it drives the
central empirical prediction ($0.919$ K-MSE ratio) tested in
\S\ref{sec:benchmarks}. Our use of Kakeya is literature-traceable
\emph{framing}, not a new harmonic-analysis theorem: the
$D_4$-lattice bound is pure lattice geometry, the direction set
$\Theta$ is a finite discrete instance (not the continuous Kakeya
hard case), and the MSE bound is multiplicative rather than
Hausdorff-dimensional.

\section{The \textsc{KakeyaLattice} codec}
\label{sec:codec}

\subsection{Nine-step pipeline, parameterised by lattice choice}

The $D_4$ nested lattice variant and the $E_8$ nested lattice
variant share the same nine-step pipeline. Only the choice of
lattice $\Lambda$ and its block dimension $\dim\Lambda$ differ.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{\textsc{KakeyaLattice} encode/decode (single vector), parametric in $\Lambda \in \{D_4, E_8\}$}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} $x \in \R^D$, $q_\mathrm{range}\in\Z_{\geq 1}$,
       lattice $\Lambda$ with $\dim\Lambda \in\{4, 8\}$,
       $D \equiv 0 \pmod{\dim\Lambda}$.
\Statex \hrulefill\ \emph{encode} \hrulefill
\State $r \gets \norm{x}_2$;\quad $u \gets x / r$ \Comment{unit-norm factorisation}
\State $y \gets H u / \sqrt{D}$ \Comment{Sylvester--Hadamard rotation}
\State $q_\mathrm{max} \gets \max_i |y_i|$ \Comment{per-vector adaptive scale}
\State $\tilde y \gets y \cdot q_\mathrm{range}/q_\mathrm{max}$ \Comment{scale onto lattice grid}
\For{each block $b = 1,\dots,D/\dim\Lambda$ of $\dim\Lambda$ coords}
  \If{$\Lambda = D_4$}
    \State $z_b \gets \mathrm{ClosestPoint}_{D_4}(\tilde y_b)$ \Comment{Conway--Sloane Alg.\ 4~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}}
  \ElsIf{$\Lambda = E_8$}
    \State $z_b \gets \mathrm{ClosestPoint}_{E_8}(\tilde y_b)$ \Comment{Conway--Sloane Alg.\ 5~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}}
  \EndIf
  \State $z_b \gets \mathrm{clamp}(z_b, -q_\mathrm{range}, +q_\mathrm{range})$
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Store:} lattice indices $\{z_b\}$ + two fp16 scalars $(r, q_\mathrm{max})$.
\Statex \hrulefill\ \emph{decode} \hrulefill
\State $\hat y \gets \{z_b\} \cdot q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$ \Comment{reconstruct rotated residual}
\State $\hat u \gets H \hat y / \sqrt{D}$ \Comment{Hadamard is self-inverse at this scale}
\State $\hat x \gets \hat u \cdot r$ \Comment{undo unit-norm factorisation}
\State \textbf{Return:} $\hat x$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{$D_4$ closest-point.}
$D_4 = \{z \in \Z^4 : \sum_i z_i \equiv 0 \pmod 2\}$. Given
$\tilde y \in \R^4$, round to $f = \mathrm{round}(\tilde y)$. If
$\sum_i f_i$ is even, $f \in D_4$. Otherwise flip the coordinate
with largest $\abs{\tilde y_i - f_i}$ by $\pm 1$ in the sign of
$\tilde y_i - f_i$. Cost: $O(1)$ per block; warp-uniform except for
a single parity-flip branch.

\paragraph{$E_8$ closest-point.}
$E_8 = D_8 \cup (D_8 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1})$ where
$D_8 = \{z \in \Z^8 : \sum_i z_i \equiv 0 \pmod 2\}$. Compute two
candidates: the closest $D_8$ point to $\tilde y$, and the closest
$D_8$ point to $\tilde y - \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1}$ translated back
by $+\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1}$; return whichever is closer to
$\tilde y$ in squared distance. Cost: two $D_8$ evaluations plus one
min, $\approx\!2.5\times$ per-block work vs $D_4$ but the block
count halves, giving a net $\approx 1.56$--$1.80\times$ encode cost
on an NVIDIA H200 (Section~\ref{sec:results-latency}).

\paragraph{Bit budget (exact vs.\ slot-packed).}
At parameter $q_\mathrm{range}$ the $D_4$ lattice contained in
$[-q_\mathrm{range},+q_\mathrm{range}]^4$ has
$(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^4/2$ points (parity constraint halves the
$\Z^4$ count), with $O(q_\mathrm{range}^3)$ boundary correction
absorbed into the clamp step. The \emph{exact} (information-theoretic)
per-block rate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bits-d4}
\text{bits per }D_4\text{ block} \;=\; 4\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1) - 1,
\end{equation}
multiplied by $K = D/4$ blocks plus two fp16 overhead scalars per
vector. Deployment slot formats address integer bits per block, so
the \emph{packed} per-block cost is
$\lceil 4\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)-1\rceil$ bits --- typically
one bit above the exact value. At $D = 128$ the $D_4$ variant at
$q_\mathrm{range}=152$ therefore uses $1056.3$ bits exact but
$33 \cdot 32 + 32 = 1088$ bits slot-packed; every bit column in
this paper's tables is the \emph{packed} value, which is what a
production PR would actually consume. The same per-$q_\mathrm{range}$
table (exact, packed, per-head bytes) is in
Appendix~\ref{app:ops}.

For the $E_8$ nested lattice variant: the two cosets (integer and
half-integer) recover the full $\Z^8$ density within a parity
constraint, so $E_8 \cap [-q_\mathrm{range},+q_\mathrm{range}]^8$
has $\approx (2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^8$ points at large
$q_\mathrm{range}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bits-e8}
\text{bits per }E_8\text{ block} \;=\; 8\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1),
\end{equation}
multiplied by $K = D/8$ blocks plus the same two fp16 scalars.
Packed (integer-aligned) per-vector cost is
$16 \lceil 8\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)\rceil + 32$. The packed
$E_8-D_4$ gap is therefore $+32$ bits per vector at low
$q_\mathrm{range}$ (parity saving visible after rounding) and
$+16$ bits at high $q_\mathrm{range}$ (parity saving absorbed into
integer alignment); the per-$q_\mathrm{range}$ gap table is in
Table~\ref{tab:v15-bits}. The exact (unpacked) $D_4 \to E_8$
overhead is the constant $+D/4$ bits per vector for the lost
parity saving, independent of $q_\mathrm{range}$; the packed gap
differs from this constant purely because ceiling arithmetic
collapses fractional bits into whole bits.

\subsection{The design role of each step}
\label{sec:codec-why}

The nine steps factor into \emph{five engineering levers plus one
lattice}. The five levers are not there for structural elegance;
each one closes a specific measured gap between real KV activations
and the i.i.d.\ isotropic source model that Shannon's
rate--distortion bound~\eqref{eq:shaping-gap} assumes. The
non-Gaussian audit of Qwen3-4B post-QK-norm $K$ summarised in
\S\ref{sec:intro-kakeya} identifies exactly four such gaps (per-vector
magnitude spread, coordinate-wise heavy tail after rotation,
per-vector envelope variability, coordinate-wise anisotropy);
against each, one of the five levers is the mechanism that closes
it. The sixth ingredient is the \emph{structural lever}: the lattice.
The $D_4$ nested lattice variant gives the fixed $+0.367\,$dB
shaping gain on top of an already-normalised input. The $E_8$ nested
lattice variant substitutes $E_8$, which is the $\R^8$ second-moment
optimum (as $D_4$ is for $\R^4$) and brings an additional
$+0.29\,$dB of theoretical shaping gain. The lattice upgrade is
strictly modular: none of the five engineering levers changes, so
the $E_8$ variant inherits every non-Gaussian-source correction the
$D_4$ variant makes, and the $E_8$ benefit stacks on top.

The five engineering levers (each closing one measured
non-Gaussian gap) are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Unit-norm factorisation} (steps 2, 12) absorbs
    per-vector magnitude spread (largest $>10\times$ median on
    every tested model), reducing the grid to the bounded
    direction $u=x/\norm{x}$ at cost of one fp16 scalar per
    vector.
  \item \textbf{Sylvester--Hadamard rotation} (steps 3, 11)
    whitens coordinate-wise anisotropy: the raw direction still
    has variance ratios up to $4.71\times$, which after
    Hadamard drops below $2\times$ (Qwen3-4B $K$). The rotation
    is arithmetic-exact in bf16~\cite{quip-hash,quip-sharp} and
    self-inverse.
  \item \textbf{Per-vector adaptive $q_\mathrm{max}$} (step 4)
    absorbs heavy per-coordinate tails; swapping per-vector for
    global scale costs $\sim\!4\times$ K-MSE.
  \item \textbf{Joint quantisation} (step 6): all $D/\dim\Lambda$
    blocks of one vector share a single $q_\mathrm{max}$, so
    bit-rate allocation tracks the per-vector envelope rather than
    block-local maxima (within-vector block variance ratios reach
    $3\times$).
  \item \textbf{Matched bit budget} (step 7 clamp plus
    \eqref{eq:bits-d4}): clamp absorbs the
    $O(q_\mathrm{range}^3)$ boundary correction. Choosing
    $q_\mathrm{range}$ to track production slot sizes
    (e.g.\ $q_\mathrm{range}=152$: exact $1056.3$ bits per
    $D=128$ vector, slot-packed to $1088$) keeps the codec
    slot-aligned; the ceiling overhead is the $3$--$11\%$
    premium discussed in \S\ref{sec:tq-comp-bits}.
\end{itemize}

The sixth ingredient is the structural lever: the lattice itself
($+0.367\,$dB from $D_4$, $+0.65\,$dB from $E_8$ over $\Z^N$,
per Prop.~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}). The five engineering levers are
already at their theoretical optima and cannot be improved
individually; the lattice is the only knob left, which is why
$D_4 \to E_8$ is our upgrade path (\S\ref{sec:v15}).

\subsection{Implementation and compliance}

Canonical Python classes \texttt{V14KakeyaZamirLatticeGPU} and
\texttt{V15KakeyaZamirE8GPU} are released under Apache-2.0 in the
\texttt{kakeyalattice} package~\cite{kakeya-v14-release}. The
codec is strict-GPU (element-wise PyTorch CUDA, no CPU
round-trip). The repository enforces a strict ban list at test
time: \textbf{no mock} (real vLLM, HF weights, WikiText-103,
FlashAttention bf16), \textbf{no simplification} (code path
identical across every model), \textbf{no fallback}
(non-divisible head dim raises \texttt{ValueError}; silent-hook
passthrough aborts via fire-count guard), \textbf{no overfit}
(boundary $k=2$ and canonical $q_\mathrm{range} \in \{10,38,152\}$
are constants across every model).

\paragraph{Snapshot-mode capture and replace.}
\label{sec:methodology-snapshot}
Two-pass protocol. \emph{Pass 1}: real vLLM prefill at
ctx~$=\!2048$; a per-architecture attention-module monkey-patch
(\texttt{snapshot\_hook.py}) captures post-QK-norm pre-RoPE $K, V$
in strict-GPU fp32. \emph{Offline}: every non-boundary layer's
$K$ (and $V$, when enabled) is codec-round-tripped on GPU.
\emph{Pass 2}: a second vLLM forward in which the same hook
replaces freshly-projected $K, V$ with the decoded tensors;
prompt-logprobs over the $n_\mathrm{eval}=64$ window $[2048,2112)$.
A fire-count guard aborts on silent passthrough. This mirrors
HuggingFace's \texttt{DynamicCache} two-pass
pattern~\cite{huggingface-transformers}: cache entries depend only
on the codec applied to the clean Pass-1 capture, isolating codec
\emph{intrinsic} error from cross-layer accumulation. Boundary
layers $\{0,1,L{-}2,L{-}1\}$ are kept bf16 by default
($L_\mathrm{bdry}/L \in \{10, 14, 17, 10\}\%$).

\paragraph{In-forward rigorous evaluation.}
\label{sec:methodology-rigorous}
Codec is applied \emph{inside} every attention step before the
KV-cache write; per-layer errors compound. Default $k=0$ (no
boundary protection); $k=2$ only for codecs structurally
undeployable at $k=0$ ($D_4$ Q=2, TQ $b=2$). Sample size $n = 32$
WikiText-103 passages per (model, codec, $q$) tuple, mean
$\pm 95\%$ CI via Student-$t$. Harness:
\texttt{benchmarks/rigorous\_eval.py}; bit-level regression
protected by a pinned \texttt{sha256} frozen-parity test
(8 hashes). Snapshot vs.\ in-forward answer different questions
(intrinsic error vs.\ deployment-realistic error); every
results-table caption cites the protocol it was produced under.

\paragraph{Quality metrics.}
$\boldsymbol{K}$ \textbf{rel-MSE} $=\!\norm{K-\hat K}_F^2/\norm{K-\bar K}_F^2$
(sample-size-independent, per-vector closed form);
$\boldsymbol{|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|}
= |\mathrm{ppl}_\mathrm{alt}-\mathrm{ppl}_\mathrm{ref}|
/\mathrm{ppl}_\mathrm{ref}$ averaged over
passages $\times n_\mathrm{eval}=64$; \textbf{top-1 pair
agreement} = fraction of eval positions where alt / ref argmax
coincide. We use $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ rather than signed
$\Delta\mathrm{ppl}$ because Hadamard-rotated structured
quantisation exhibits a de-biasing
phenomenon~\cite{quip-sharp} on well-regularised models, so the
signed value crosses zero and is not monotone in codec quality.

\section{Measured performance of the $E_8$ nested lattice variant}
\label{sec:v15}

The codec is parametric in $\Lambda \in \{D_4, E_8\}$; the $E_8$
variant differs only in the closest-point subroutine. Every result
in this section uses the in-forward rigorous protocol
(\S\ref{sec:methodology-rigorous}; codec applied inside every
attention step, no-boundary unless stated, $n=32$, $95\%$ CI) ---
deliberately harsher than \S\ref{sec:benchmarks}'s snapshot
protocol, because the $E_8$ upgrade's practical benefit manifests
only under cross-layer error accumulation. $E_8$'s Voronoi cell is
closer to a sphere than $D_4$'s, giving $+0.29\,$dB theoretical
shaping gain on top of the five engineering levers.

Two $E_8$ design facts: (i) Conway--Sloane Alg.~5 computes two
$D_8$ candidates (integer + half-integer cosets) per block, at
$\approx 2.5\times$ the per-block cost of $D_4$'s single
parity-flip; $E_8$ operates in $8$-D blocks so the block count
halves, giving a net $1.56$--$1.80\times$ encode-decode latency
ratio (Table~\ref{tab:latency}). (ii) $E_8$'s two-coset union
recovers full $\Z^8$ density, losing $D_4$'s $-1$-bit parity
saving. Exact-rate cost is $+D/4 = +32$ bits/vector at every
$q_\mathrm{range}$; packed (slot-aligned) cost is $+32$ at low
$q_\mathrm{range}$ narrowing to $+16$ at high $q_\mathrm{range}$
as the parity saving is absorbed into ceiling arithmetic
(Table~\ref{tab:v15-bits}; CR cost $-5$--$7\%$ at
iso-$q_\mathrm{range}$).

\section{Caveats and known limits}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Snapshot ($n=4$) vs.\ in-forward rigorous ($n=32$).}
    The snapshot $D_4$ tables in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks} use
    $n=4$ passages; K-MSE is sample-size-independent but
    $\abs{\Delta\text{ppl}}$ below $\sim\!1\%$ absolute is inside
    the FlashAttention bf16 reproducibility floor and should be
    read as a tie. The in-forward rigorous tables in
    \S\ref{sec:v15} use $n=32$ and $95\%$ CI to eliminate this
    confound for the $E_8$-vs-$D_4$ comparison. The two protocols
    answer different questions (intrinsic vs.\ deployment-realistic
    error); PPL in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks} is a lower bound on
    in-forward deployment PPL; K-MSE and shaping-gain ratios are
    not affected by protocol choice.

  \item \textbf{Head dimension divisibility.} The $D_4$ codec
    raises \texttt{ValueError} on $d_h \not\equiv 0 \pmod 4$; the
    $E_8$ codec on $d_h \not\equiv 0 \pmod 8$. Models with
    $d_h \in \{96, 176\}$ need a different block structure.

  \item \textbf{KV-sharing layers.} Gemma-4-E4B's
    \texttt{kv\_sharing\_target\_layer\_name} layers are excluded
    from capture (they re-use another layer's KV cache), matching
    the model's native semantics.

  \item \textbf{Kakeya lower bound is conditional at $D\geq 4$.}
    Proposition~\ref{prop:kakeya-rd} remains conditional on the
    Kakeya-maximal-function conjecture at $D\geq 4$. At $D=3$ the
    lower bound is unconditional via Wang--Zahl~\cite{wang-zahl}; at
    deployment head dimensions it is a conditional framing, not a
    proof of Pareto-optimality. The \emph{$D_4$ shaping bound} in
    Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya} is unconditional, but it
    applies to the $4$-dimensional block structure of the codec
    rather than to the full head dimension.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Comparison with TurboQuant}
\label{sec:tq-comparison}

TurboQuant~\cite{turboquant-vllm,vllm-pr-38479} is the strongest
production-deployed KV codec; its $k8v4$ preset rotates by
Sylvester--Hadamard, unit-norm factorises, then quantises $K$ at
FP8~E4M3 scalar + $V$ at 4-bit uniform, giving $1056$
bits/token/head at $D=128$ ($8\cdot 128 + 2\cdot\mathrm{fp16}$).
TurboQuant and \textsc{KakeyaLattice} share four levers (unit-norm
factorisation, Hadamard rotation, per-vector $q_\mathrm{max}$,
joint scale) and differ only in the per-block quantiser:
TurboQuant uses int-per-coord $\Z^4$ cells ($G=1/12$, $0\,$dB
reference), \textsc{KakeyaLattice} uses $D_4$ closest-point
($G\approx 0.0766$, $+0.367\,$dB) --- a drop-in change.

\paragraph{Near-matched rate: the $3$--$11\%$ premium.}
\label{sec:tq-comp-bits}
At each $q$-tier the $D_4$ variant's packed rate sits $3$--$11\%$
above its paired TurboQuant-$b$ rate because
$4\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)-1$ rounds up to the next integer
(TurboQuant's per-coord uniform bits are integer by construction).
At $q_\mathrm{range}=152$ the $D_4$ exact rate is $1056.3$ bits
per $D=128$ vector but the packed rate is $1088$, paired against
TQ $k8v4$ at exactly $1056$. The three canonical operating points
sit at packed-rate premia of $+11\%$ ($q_\mathrm{range}=10$, $576$
vs.\ $\sim\!520$ interpolated TQ), $+4\%$ ($q_\mathrm{range}=38$,
$832$ vs.\ $800$ at TQ $b=6$), and $+3\%$ ($q_\mathrm{range}=152$,
$1088$ vs.\ $1056$ at TQ $b=8$). The Pareto comparisons in
Tables~\ref{tab:pareto-qwen3}, \ref{tab:multimodel},
\ref{tab:theory-vs-exp} are therefore near-matched rate, not
iso-rate: at a $3$--$11\%$ packed-rate premium,
\textsc{KakeyaLattice} delivers $8$--$36\%$ better K-MSE and a
strictly better Pareto curve.

\paragraph{Proposed upstream PR.}
A Triton kernel for Conway--Sloane $D_4$ closest-point
($\sim\!10$ lines element-wise); slot-format alignment ($32$
blocks $\times 33$ bits + $2$ fp16 scalars, bit-compatible with
$k8v4$); new \texttt{--kv-cache-dtype} preset
\texttt{turboquant\_d4v4} alongside \texttt{turboquant\_k8v4}. The
change is additive. End-to-end vLLM throughput measurement
requires paged-attention-native kernel integration and is out of
scope for this paper.
\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Kakeya problem and harmonic analysis.}
The Kakeya problem was posed in 1917~\cite{kakeya-problem};
Besicovitch gave the first non-trivial
constructions~\cite{besicovitch-1919,besicovitch-1928}; Fefferman
and C\'ordoba connected it to oscillatory
integrals~\cite{fefferman-ball,cordoba-multiplier}; Bourgain,
Wolff, Katz--{\L}aba--Tao, and Tao advanced high-$D$ and
$\R^3$ bounds~\cite{bourgain-kakeya,wolff-kakeya,katz-laba-tao,katz-tao-discretized,tao-kakeya-survey};
Dvir~\cite{dvir-finite-field,dvir-kopparty-saraf-sudan} resolved
the finite-field analogue via the polynomial method;
Bennett--Carbery--Tao, Guth, and Guth--Katz developed the
multilinear Kakeya / polynomial-partitioning
chain~\cite{bennett-carbery-tao,guth-endpoint,guth-katz};
Hickman--Rogers--Zhang and Zahl pushed high-$D$
bounds~\cite{hickman-rogers-zhang,zahl-restricted,zahl-2022};
Wang--Zahl~\cite{wang-zahl} resolved the three-dimensional
Euclidean case in 2025. The paper relies on three specific links:
Guth's multilinear Kakeya endpoint~\cite{guth-endpoint},
Brascamp--Lieb~\cite{bennett-carbery-christ-tao,carbery-valdimarsson},
and Tropp's matrix-concentration
bounds~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff}.

\paragraph{Lattice source coding.}
Zamir--Feder~\cite{zamir-feder} established the
rate--distortion framework; Conway--Sloane
\cite{conway-sloane-d4,conway-sloane-book} gave the $D_4$
second-moment optimality and the closest-point algorithm.
Eyuboglu--Forney~\cite{eyuboglu-forney},
Forney~\cite{forney-trellis}, and
Erez--Zamir~\cite{erez-zamir} developed lattice source/channel
coding; QuIP\#~\cite{quip-sharp} applied lattice codebooks to
LLM weight quantisation. The Halko--Martinsson--Tropp randomised
SVD~\cite{halko-martinsson-tropp} closes the Brascamp--Lieb to
matrix-concentration arc numerically and grounded our prior
work~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper}.

\paragraph{KV-cache compression.}
Token eviction (H$_2$O~\cite{h2o},
Scissorhands~\cite{scissorhands},
StreamingLLM~\cite{streaming-llm}) reduces $T$, orthogonal to a
codec. Per-channel quantisation (KIVI~\cite{kivi}) sits outside
the Hadamard-rotation stack. Hadamard-based codecs include
\emph{Cache Me If You Must}~\cite{shutova-cache} and
TurboQuant~\cite{turboquant-vllm,vllm-pr-38479,vllm-pr-40194,vllm-pr-39953,vllm-pr-40060,vllm-pr-40122},
our direct comparison baseline (\S\ref{sec:tq-comparison}).
Trained low-rank methods (DMC~\cite{dmc}, LESS~\cite{less}) are
orthogonal to post-hoc codecs. Our prior
work~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper} used an RSVD + Lloyd-Max codec with a
Kakeya-conditional rank bound; the present codec replaces that
conjectural bound with the unconditional $D_4$ shaping bound of
Prop.~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}.

\paragraph{Infrastructure.}
vLLM~\cite{vllm} is the reference serving stack;
transformers~\cite{huggingface-transformers} supplies weights and
tokenisers; FlashAttention~\cite{flashattention} is the default
attention kernel.
\section{Conclusion}

\textsc{KakeyaLattice} is a Zamir--Feder nested-lattice KV-cache
codec with two lattice variants. The $D_4$ variant's shaping gain
is unconditionally bounded by $G(\Z^4)/G(D_4)\approx 0.919$ via
Prop.~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}; placing it at the shallow end of the
multilinear Kakeya $\to$ Brascamp--Lieb $\to$ matrix concentration
$\to$ lattice second-moment chain
(\S\ref{sec:framework})~\cite{guth-endpoint,bennett-carbery-christ-tao,tropp-matrix-chernoff,conway-sloane-book,wang-zahl}
grounds the design in published theorems without a conditional
Kakeya dependency at deployment dimension. Under snapshot
evaluation the theoretical $0.919$ ratio is recovered within
$\sim\!1\%$ on every tested model in three independent
environments; the codec wins $12/12$ on K-MSE and $9/12$ on
$|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ across four transformer families, and
shrinks the $128$k KV cache by $2.5$--$2.8\times$ balanced and
$1.66$--$1.73\times$ near-lossless with both $K$ and $V$
compressed. The $E_8$ variant extends this with $+1.3$ to
$+2.0\,$dB further per-layer K-MSE gain and a $28$--$53\%$
in-forward $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ reduction (four models,
$n=32$, $95\%$ CI); the excess over the $+0.29\,$dB theoretical
minimum is super-linear amplification of per-layer error under
cross-layer accumulation. Cost is $+16$--$32$ packed bits/vector
and $1.56$--$1.80\times$ encode latency; long-context retrieval
is preserved on three of four models. We frame the codec as an
additive near-matched-rate drop-in PR over the production-deployed
TurboQuant kernel (\S\ref{sec:tq-comparison}).

\paragraph{Reproducibility and distortion choice.}
All code, unit tests, per-model JSON, four per-architecture
attention hooks, and the multi-model / NIAH / latency harnesses
are released under Apache-2.0 in \texttt{kakeyalattice}
($D_4$: \texttt{V14KakeyaZamirLatticeGPU};
$E_8$: \texttt{V15KakeyaZamirE8GPU}); full manifest
\S\ref{app:repro}. We report MSE rather than inner-product
distortion because $|q^\top(k-\hat k)| \leq \norm{k-\hat k}$ for
unit-norm $q$, so MSE is a conservative upper bound on
attention-logit perturbation; measured $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ and
top-1 agreement confirm this proxy is tight in practice.

# PPL / MSE / CR (v1.4 + v1.5 + TurboQuant at matched Q / b):

    --model-path <HF-id> --model-name <short>_nobdry \
    --mode inforward --no-boundary \
    --q-values 4,10 --v15-q-values 4,10 --tq-b-values 3 \
    --kv-modes KV \
    --ctx-len 2048 --n-eval 64 --n-passages 32 \
    --out-dir reports/v1_5_release

python benchmarks/rigorous_eval.py \
    --model-path <HF-id> --model-name <short>_tqb2_bdry2 \
    --mode inforward --boundary-size 2 \
    --q-values "" --v15-q-values "" --tq-b-values 2 \
    --kv-modes KV \
    --ctx-len 2048 --n-eval 64 --n-passages 32 \
    --out-dir reports/v1_5_release

# Frozen sha256 parity (bit-level regression gate):

For Gemma-4-E4B's heterogeneous head\_dim (20 sliding-attention
layers with $d_h = 256$, 4 full-attention layers with $d_h = 512$),

\paragraph{Deployment recommendation.} Use
\textbf{$E_8$ Q=$10$} (CR $3.37\times$, $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$
typically $1.6$--$6.9\%$) as the default head-of-line operating
point. Use $E_8$ Q=$4$ (CR $4.57\times$) when aggressive compression
is non-negotiable and $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ of $6$--$32\%$ is
acceptable on the target model family. Use $E_8$ Q=$152$
(CR $1.86\times$, $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}| < 1\%$) when near-lossless
is required. Stay on the $D_4$ nested lattice variant for latency-critical
paths where the $1.56$--$1.80\times$ $E_8$ encode cost is
unacceptable.
