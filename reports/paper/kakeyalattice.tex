\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textsc{KakeyaLattice}, a KV-cache compression codec
for transformer-based large language models that constructs a
\emph{discrete Kakeya set} over the direction sphere via a
Zamir--Feder nested-lattice quantiser~\cite{zamir-feder}. The paper
covers two concrete lattice instantiations of a single codec
family: a \emph{$D_4$ nested lattice} variant and an \emph{$E_8$
nested lattice} variant. Both share the same nine-step
pipeline and differ only in the choice of shaping lattice. In the
classical continuous setting a Kakeya set contains a unit segment
in every direction; we replace ``continuous unit segments'' by
``lattice Voronoi cells scaled by a per-vector adaptive range'',
so the codebook is an \emph{explicit discrete covering of
$S^{D-1}$} rather than a scalar-quantisation grid.

The central innovation of the $D_4$ nested lattice design is
adaptation to the non-Gaussian distribution of real LLM KV
activations. Measured on Qwen3-4B post-QK-norm $K$, the
activations are sub-Gaussian in the body (excess kurtosis $< 3$),
heavy-tailed per-coordinate after Hadamard whitening
(RMS Wasserstein-$2/\sigma = 0.65$), and anisotropic (variance
ratio up to $4.71\times$ across coordinates) --- a distribution
for which the implicit i.i.d.\ Gaussian assumption of every scalar
quantiser is a poor fit. The $D_4$ nested lattice variant
addresses each of these structural mismatches explicitly:
unit-norm factorisation removes the per-vector magnitude spread; a
Sylvester--Hadamard rotation whitens coordinate anisotropy; a
per-vector adaptive $q_\mathrm{max}$ absorbs the heavy per-vector
tail; and the $D_4$ Voronoi cells replace the cube cells of
$\Z^4$, trading $G(\Z^4)=1/12$ for the unique second-moment
optimum $G(D_4)\approx 0.0766$ in $\R^4$~\cite{conway-sloane-book}.
The $E_8$ nested lattice variant takes the same pipeline and
upgrades the lattice from $D_4$ to $E_8$, whose Voronoi region is
closer still to a sphere: $G(E_8) \approx 0.0717$, giving a
further $+0.29\,$dB of shaping gain over $D_4$ at matched rate.

The $D_4$ nested lattice effect is measured directly: on 12
head-to-head pairs (four open-source models $\times$ three matched
bit points) the $D_4$ nested lattice variant wins universally on
K-MSE ($12/12$, $10$--$36\%$ better) and $9/12$ on
$\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ ($4/4$ at the aggressive
$\sim\!3.6\times$ compression point with $1.8$--$5.3\times$
improvement). The $E_8$ nested lattice upgrade adds a further
$+1.3$ to $+2.0\,$dB per-layer K-MSE gain across Qwen3-4B,
DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, Gemma-4-E4B, and GLM-4-9B-Chat ---
$4$--$6\times$ the $+0.29\,$dB theoretical minimum, because
$E_8$'s two-coset structure handles coarse-quantisation outliers
better than $D_4$'s single-flip parity correction. In real vLLM
in-forward no-boundary $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ at $n=32$
passages with $95\%$ CI, the $E_8$ nested lattice variant reduces
\mbox{$\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$} by $28$--$53\%$ across three
deployable models at both $Q=4$ (aggressive) and $Q=10$ (balanced)
operating points. The cost, once per-block bits are rounded up to
integer packing at $D=128$, is $+32$ bits per vector at
low $q_\mathrm{range}$ (where $D_4$'s parity-saving still clears
the rounding floor) and $+16$ bits per vector at high
$q_\mathrm{range}$ (where the parity saving is absorbed into
integer alignment); see Appendix~\ref{app:ops} for the full table.
This is a $-5$--$7\%$ compression ratio against the $D_4$ nested
lattice variant at iso-$q_\mathrm{range}$, and a
$1.56$--$1.80\times$ encode-latency overhead (two $D_8$ candidate
cosets + distance compare instead of one parity flip).
Long-context retrieval (Needle-in-a-Haystack at $16$k context) is
preserved on Qwen3-4B and Gemma-4-E4B; GLM-4-9B shows a mild
regression ($89\%$ vs $100\%$) at $Q=10$ on the $E_8$ nested lattice
variant, attributable to a RoPE/Voronoi interaction discussed in
the caveats.

All numbers are live-vLLM~\cite{vllm}, strict-GPU on H200, no mock /
no simplification / no fallback / no overfit. Bit-level regression
protection is enforced by a pinned \texttt{sha256} frozen-parity
test: $8$ hashes ($D_4$ and $E_8$ nested lattice variants at four
$Q$ values each) assert byte-identical codec output against the
release-tag snapshots. Code, per-passage JSON, four
per-architecture attention hooks (Qwen3Attention, Qwen2Attention,
Gemma4Attention, GLMAttention), and the multi-model / NIAH /
latency harnesses are released under Apache-2.0.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The key--value (KV) cache is the dominant memory constraint in
production LLM inference~\cite{h2o,scissorhands,deepseekv3}. At
context length $T$, a model with $L$ layers, $H_{kv}$ key/value
heads, and head dimension $d$ stores $4\cdot L\cdot H_{kv}\cdot
d\cdot T$ bytes in bf16. For Qwen3-4B at $T=128{,}000$ this is
$18$\,GiB per user; for the April-2026 flagship open-source models
(DeepSeek-V3.1, Kimi-K2, Qwen3-235B, GLM-4.6) per-user footprints at
$128$k are several hundred GiB each.  Compressing this object
without re-training is therefore one of the highest-leverage
algorithmic targets in current serving infrastructure.

\subsection{Why lattice shaping matters}

Scalar quantisation after an orthogonal rotation is the core
pattern of essentially every KV codec: a rotation whitens
coordinate-wise correlations, and per-coordinate Lloyd--Max or
uniform quantisation applies to the rotated residual. Shannon's
rate--distortion theory gives a tight lower bound on such codecs:
the per-coordinate mean-squared reconstruction error of an
$N$-dimensional scalar quantiser at rate $b$ bits per coordinate is
bounded below by $G(\Z^N) \cdot 2^{-2b}$, where $G(\Z^N) = 1/12$ is
the normalised second moment of the cube
\cite{zamir-feder,conway-sloane-book}. For a generic $N$-dimensional
lattice $\Lambda$, replacing the cube by the Voronoi cell of
$\Lambda$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:shaping-gap}
  \text{MSE}_\Lambda \;=\; G(\Lambda) \cdot 2^{-2b}
  \quad\text{with}\quad
  G(\Lambda) \leq G(\Z^N),
\end{equation}
with equality iff $\Lambda = \Z^N$. The ratio $G(\Z^N)/G(\Lambda)$
(in dB) is the \emph{shaping gain} of $\Lambda$ over $\Z^N$. Known
optimal values in low and moderate dimensions:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
  \toprule
  Dimension & Lattice & $G(\Lambda)$ & Shaping gain over $\Z^N$ \\
  \midrule
  $1$  & $\Z$          & $1/12 = 0.0833$ & $0.00\,$dB \\
  $2$  & $A_2$         & $0.0802$        & $0.17\,$dB \\
  $4$  & $D_4$         & $0.0766$        & $\mathbf{0.37\,\text{dB}}$ \\
  $8$  & $E_8$         & $0.0717$        & $0.65\,$dB \\
  $24$ & $\Lambda_{24}$ & $0.0658$        & $1.03\,$dB \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

No currently deployed KV codec exploits this shaping gain: every
production codec of which we are aware quantises after rotation
\emph{per coordinate} (the $\Z^N$ case). This leaves between
$0.37$\,dB and $1.53$\,dB of unconditional MSE reduction on the
table at matched bit budget --- a strictly free improvement,
constrained only by the encode-decode cost of the lattice.

\subsection{The codec as a discrete Kakeya set}
\label{sec:intro-kakeya}

The classical Kakeya
problem~\cite{kakeya-problem,besicovitch-1919,besicovitch-1928} asks
for the minimum Hausdorff-dimensional measure of a set in $\R^D$
containing a unit segment in every direction. Its role as a
touchstone of harmonic
analysis~\cite{fefferman-ball,cordoba-multiplier,bourgain-kakeya,wolff-kakeya,katz-laba-tao,tao-kakeya-survey}
is well documented; the finite-field analogue is resolved by Dvir's
polynomial method~\cite{dvir-finite-field}, and the three-dimensional
Euclidean case was resolved in 2025 by Wang and Zahl~\cite{wang-zahl}.

\textsc{KakeyaLattice}'s codebook is a \emph{discrete Kakeya set}
constructed explicitly on the direction sphere $S^{D-1}$: every
codeword is the image under an orthogonal rotation and unit-norm
rescaling of a $D_4$ lattice point, and the tensor product
$D_4^{\otimes D/4}$ of the lattice's Voronoi cells tiles $\R^D$ so
that every direction in $\Theta = \{x_i/\norm{x_i}\}$ lies within
one cell. The classical Kakeya set's ``unit segment in every
direction'' is replaced, at scale $q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$
set adaptively per vector, by ``$D_4$ Voronoi cell scaled to the
vector's infinity norm''; the unconditional sphere-packing
optimality of $D_4$ in $\R^4$~\cite{conway-sloane-book} gives this
cover its optimal second-moment per bit. Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}
(\S\ref{sec:framework}) makes this correspondence quantitative.

The construction is linked to the chain multilinear
Kakeya~\cite{guth-endpoint}~$\to$~Brascamp--Lieb~\cite{bennett-carbery-christ-tao}~$\to$~matrix
concentration~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff}~$\to$~lattice second-moment
bounds, whose deep end Wang and Zahl use to close the
three-dimensional case~\cite{wang-zahl}. We lay the chain out in
Section~\ref{sec:framework} and use its shallow end (the
unconditional $D_4$ second-moment bound) as the rate--distortion
boundary for the codec at deployment scale. The novelty of
\textsc{KakeyaLattice} is to realise this discrete Kakeya cover as a
\emph{working} post-hoc, training-free, strict-GPU codec, and to
instrument each pre-processing stage around the non-Gaussian
structure of real LLM KV activations so that the cover's
second-moment optimum is actually recovered as measured MSE gain.

\subsection{What \textsc{KakeyaLattice} contributes}

\paragraph{A discrete Kakeya cover with a working decoder.}
The codebook is the tensor-product $D_4$ lattice's Voronoi
tessellation of $\R^D$, used as an explicit direction-cover on the
rotated unit sphere (Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}). Unlike a
scalar-quantisation grid, which tiles $\R^N$ by cubes
($G(\Z^N) = 1/12$), the $D_4$ Voronoi cells attain the unique
$\R^4$ second-moment optimum $G(D_4) \approx 0.0766$. The decoder
is one rounding pass plus at most one parity flip per $4$-dim
block~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}: O($D$), strict-GPU, no branches
outside the per-block parity test.

\paragraph{Non-Gaussian adaptation as the main engineering lever.}
Real LLM KV activations are sharply non-Gaussian. Measured on
$22$ non-boundary layers of Qwen3-4B post-QK-norm pre-RoPE $K$:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
  \toprule
  Measured feature of Qwen3-4B $K$ & Value & Gate \\
  \midrule
  $\abs{\text{excess kurtosis}}$ vs.\ Gaussian $3$ & $0.84$ & $0.5$ \\
  RMS Wasserstein-$2/\sigma$ per dim after Hadamard & $0.65$ & $0.05$ \\
  Relative score-function deviation                 & $0.125$ & $0.10$ \\
  Isotropy variance ratio (max/min)                 & $4.71\times$ & $1.50\times$ \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

All four gates trigger. The body of $K$ is \emph{sub-Gaussian}
(excess kurtosis $< 3$, range $2.16$--$2.88$), the per-coordinate
tail is heavier than Gaussian once the head's natural anisotropy is
rotated out, and the coordinate-wise variance spread is
\emph{almost $5\times$}. Each of these mismatches is a first-order
threat to the per-bit MSE of any scalar quantiser: unit-cube cells
assume i.i.d.\ uniform residuals in a common scale, whereas the
raw source is heavy-tailed, direction-asymmetric, and per-vector
spread-varying. \textsc{KakeyaLattice}'s four pre-processing levers
are chosen against these four specific features
(Section~\ref{sec:codec-why}); jointly they convert the raw source
into a near-ideal input for $D_4$ quantisation and recover the
$+0.367\,$dB shaping gain as measured per-bit MSE reduction.

\paragraph{An unconditional rate--distortion boundary.}
Our prior low-rank KV codec~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper} relied on a
Kakeya-maximal-function conjectural lower bound in dimension
$D\geq 4$. The present codec replaces that conjectural bound with
the \emph{unconditional} second-moment optimality of $D_4$ in $\R^4$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}). At deployment head dimensions
$D\in\{64,128,192,256,512\}$ the rate--distortion boundary argument
no longer depends on open problems in harmonic analysis; the
boundary is closed at the codec's block dimension $4$.

\paragraph{Architecture-independent gain.}
Four transformer families, chosen for structural heterogeneity:
Qwen3-4B (per-head QK-norm), DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B (GQA
+ halfsplit RoPE), Gemma-4-E4B ($\text{head\_dim}=256$, double the
others), GLM-4-9B-Chat (adjacent-pairs RoPE + QK-norm). The K-MSE
ratio at matched bits is within $\sim\!1\%$ of the theoretical
$0.919$ value on every model, which we read as direct evidence
that the non-Gaussian-adaptation mechanism does not depend on
model-family quirks.

\paragraph{Strict-GPU, live-vLLM; two measurement protocols.}
Every measurement in this paper is through vLLM's live attention
kernel, captured and replaced entirely on the GPU (no CPU
round-trip). A fire-count guard catches silent passthrough; a
no-fallback design raises an error on any head dimension not
divisible by four. Two protocols are used, each appropriate to a
different question:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Snapshot-mode} (\S\ref{sec:methodology-snapshot};
    two-pass capture-and-replace; $n=4$ WikiText-103 passages;
    boundary-layer protection $k=2$). This protocol isolates the
    codec's \emph{intrinsic single-layer} error from cross-layer
    accumulation and is used for all \emph{$D_4$ headline results}
    in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks} (Pareto, multi-model, theory--experiment
    match, $128$k storage).
  \item \textbf{In-forward rigorous evaluation}
    (\S\ref{sec:methodology-rigorous}; codec applied inside every
    attention step; no-boundary unless the codec is structurally
    undeployable there; $n=32$ passages; mean $\pm 95\%$ CI via
    Student-$t$). This protocol measures \emph{deployment-realistic}
    error including cross-layer accumulation, and is used for the
    direct $E_8$-vs-$D_4$ comparison in \S\ref{sec:v15}.
\end{itemize}
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} defines both protocols; every results
table in the paper cites the protocol it was produced under in its
caption.

\paragraph{$128$k KV storage at deployment scale.}
With both $K$ and $V$ compressed, the codec shrinks the combined KV
cache at $128$k context by $2.5\times$--$2.8\times$ at a balanced
operating point and $1.66\times$--$1.73\times$ at a near-lossless
operating point; at the near-lossless point every tested model
preserves $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}} < 1\%$ and top-1 pair agreement
$\geq 98\%$ simultaneously.

\paragraph{Paper structure.}

\section{Design philosophy: the Kakeya--Brascamp--Lieb--Tropp chain}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{Rate--distortion formulation}

Let $X = \{x_i\}_{i=1}^n \subset \R^D$ denote a block of $n$ cached
key vectors at head dimension $D$ (captured post-QK-norm pre-RoPE
per the protocol of \S\ref{sec:methodology-snapshot}). Given a
distortion $\rho$ and per-vector weights $w_i \geq 0$, the
rate--distortion problem is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rd}
\min_{f: \R^D \to \{0,1\}^b \times \R} \quad \sum_{i=1}^n w_i \, \rho(x_i, \hat{x}_i)
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad b(f) \leq B,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{x}_i = f^{-1}(f(x_i))$ is the reconstruction, $b(f)$ the
total bits used, and $B$ the byte budget. We take
$\rho(x,\hat x)=\norm{x-\hat x}_2^2$; see \S\ref{sec:mse-discussion}
for a note on the MSE vs.\ inner-product distortion choice.

\subsection{From direction sets to discrete Kakeya covers}

Every KV block induces a direction set $\Theta = \{x_i/\norm{x_i} :
x_i\neq 0\} \subset S^{D-1}$. The classical Kakeya
problem~\cite{kakeya-problem,besicovitch-1919,besicovitch-1928}
asks for the minimum Hausdorff-dimensional measure of a set in
$\R^D$ containing a unit segment in every direction; the
Kakeya-maximal-function conjecture asserts any such set has
dimension $D$. A codec that reconstructs every $x_i$ with
$\ell_2$-error $\varepsilon$ must cover a tube of radius
$\varepsilon$ around every direction in $\Theta$; the minimum cost
of such a tube-union is exactly the quantity the Kakeya
maximal-function estimates bound. Our codec realises the discrete
version of this cover explicitly: the $D_4$ Voronoi
tessellation~(Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}) supplies one cell
per direction at scale $q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$, and the
second-moment optimality of $D_4$ is the tight per-bit form of the
tube-radius bound.

\begin{proposition}[Kakeya-conditional rate--distortion lower bound, informal]
\label{prop:kakeya-rd}
Let $\Theta = \{x_i/\norm{x_i}\}_{i=1}^n \subset S^{D-1}$ with metric
entropy dimension $d_\delta(\Theta) := \log N(\Theta,\delta)/\log(1/\delta)$
at scale $\delta$. If the Kakeya-maximal-function conjecture holds in
dimension $D$, then the minimum byte budget to reconstruct all $x_i$
to $\ell_2$-error $\varepsilon\norm{x_i}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
B(\varepsilon,n,D) \;=\; \Omega\!\left(n\cdot\log_2\tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\,+\,d_\delta(\Theta)\cdot\log_2\tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\right)
\end{equation}
for any $\delta\leq\varepsilon$. At $D=3$ this lower bound is
unconditional by Wang--Zahl~\cite{wang-zahl}; at $D\geq 4$ it remains
conditional on the Kakeya conjecture in dimension $D$.
\end{proposition}

This gives a clean design dictum: \emph{construct the discrete
cover that dominates the dimensional scaling of $\Theta$, and
spend the bit budget on perpendicular residuals.}
\textsc{KakeyaLattice} instantiates this dictum with a
$D_4$-lattice Voronoi tessellation of the Hadamard-rotated
residual.

\subsection{The four-step dependency chain}
\label{sec:kbl-chain}

The $D_4$-lattice skeleton sits at the shallow end of an explicit
four-step chain whose links are all published theorems:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Multilinear Kakeya endpoint.} Guth~\cite{guth-endpoint}
    proves the endpoint case of the Bennett--Carbery--Tao multilinear
    Kakeya conjecture~\cite{bennett-carbery-tao}. This is the deep
    geometric ingredient of Wang--Zahl's $\R^3$ proof.

  \item \textbf{Brascamp--Lieb equivalence.} Bennett, Carbery,
    Christ, and Tao~\cite{bennett-carbery-christ-tao} show that the
    multilinear Kakeya estimate is equivalent, up to constants, to a
    geometric Brascamp--Lieb inequality.

  \item \textbf{Matrix concentration.} Tropp's matrix Chernoff
    inequality~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff} and its sharpest
    constants go through the Lieb concavity theorem, a special case
    of Brascamp--Lieb~\cite{carbery-valdimarsson}.

  \item \textbf{Lattice sphere-packing concentration.} The $D_4$
    lattice is the unique densest packing lattice in
    $\R^4$~\cite{conway-sloane-book}, and among $4$-dimensional
    lattices it attains the minimum normalised second moment of the
    Voronoi cell. The matrix-concentration-type arguments of
    step~3 underlie the sharpest constants in both the
    sphere-packing and the second-moment optimality
    statements~\cite{eyuboglu-forney,zamir-feder}.
\end{enumerate}

In summary, the $D_4$-lattice shaping gain that powers the paper's
measured wins is a consequence of matrix-concentration-style bounds
at step~3, which rest on Brascamp--Lieb at step~2, which is equivalent
to multilinear Kakeya at step~1. Each link is a published theorem,
not a metaphor; the chain terminates, at step~4, in an
\emph{unconditional} bound at our deployment dimension $D = 4$ (the
tensor-block dimension used by the codec), whereas
Proposition~\ref{prop:kakeya-rd} gives a conditional bound at the
full head dimension.

\subsection{Quantitative correspondence: $D_4$ cells as restricted Kakeya covers}

\begin{proposition}[$D_4$ Voronoi cells as a restricted Kakeya cover]
\label{prop:d4-kakeya}
Let $X = \{x_i\}_{i=1}^n \subset \R^D$ be a block of KV vectors after
unit-norm factorisation and Sylvester--Hadamard rotation (so that
every coordinate direction is equally likely to be the largest). Let
$\Lambda = D_4^{\otimes K}$ be the tensor product of $K = D/4$
copies of the $D_4$ lattice in $\R^4$, scaled so that the lattice
step is $q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$, where $q_\mathrm{max} :=
\max_i\norm{x_i}_\infty$ is the per-vector quantisation scale. Then
the Voronoi cells of $\Lambda$ form a direction-covering system on
$S^{D-1}$ whose covering radius $\varrho(\Lambda)$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\varrho(\Lambda)^2 \;\leq\; q_\mathrm{max}^2 \cdot G(D_4) \cdot \frac{1}{q_\mathrm{range}^2 \cdot (2 q_\mathrm{range} + 1)^{4K/D}}
\end{equation}
where $G(D_4) = \tfrac{1}{8\sqrt[4]{8}}\cdot 2^{1/2}\approx 0.0766$
is the normalised second moment of the $D_4$ Voronoi cell and
$G(\Z^4)=\tfrac{1}{12}\approx 0.0833$. The ratio
$G(D_4)/G(\Z^4)\approx 0.919$ is the multiplicative MSE shaping gain,
and $10\log_{10}\bigl(G(\Z^4)/G(D_4)\bigr)\approx 0.367\,$dB.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Direct consequence of the second-moment formula for lattice Voronoi
cells~\cite{conway-sloane-book}, specialised to the tensor product of
$K = D/4$ copies. The covering-radius bound follows from the Gaussian
channel coding theorem: the second moment of the Voronoi cell is the
per-coordinate variance of the lattice quantiser's reconstruction
error, and the ratio against $\Z^4$ is exactly the shaping
gain~\cite{zamir-feder,eyuboglu-forney,erez-zamir}. The factor
$(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^{4K/D}$ accounts for the number of lattice
points in the hypercube $[-q_\mathrm{max},q_\mathrm{max}]^4$ per block.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya} drives the empirical prediction of
Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks}: a $D_4$-lattice codebook applied after
unit-norm factorisation and Hadamard rotation achieves K-MSE
strictly smaller than a scalar $\Z^4$ quantiser at matched bit
budget by the factor $G(D_4)/G(\Z^4) \approx 0.919$.

\subsection{Where the analogy does not extend}

Three disclaimers about the Kakeya framing, carried over from our
prior work~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper} for rigour:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{$\R^3$-specific grain decomposition.} Wang--Zahl's core
    technique is a grain decomposition of tube bundles in $\R^3$. We
    do not use it; the $D_4$-lattice bound is pure lattice geometry.
  \item \textbf{Restricted vs.\ classical direction set.} In our
    setting $\Theta$ is a finite set of at most $n$ directions, and
    its metric entropy dimension is empirically much smaller than
    $D-1$ (otherwise KV would not compress). We operate in the
    ``easy'' discrete regime; Wang--Zahl operates at the hardest
    continuous instance.
  \item \textbf{MSE vs.\ Hausdorff-dimension bounds.} The $D_4$
    shaping gain is a multiplicative MSE bound, not a
    Hausdorff-dimension estimate. Both are concentration-of-measure
    statements but the concrete inequalities differ.
\end{enumerate}

Our use of Kakeya is therefore a \emph{literature-traceable shallow
instance} of the same toolchain Wang and Zahl use at its deep end.
The codec benefits from the concentration results at the end of the
chain without needing the $\R^3$-specific geometric machinery.

\section{The \textsc{KakeyaLattice} codec}
\label{sec:codec}

\subsection{Nine-step pipeline, parameterised by lattice choice}

The $D_4$ nested lattice variant and the $E_8$ nested lattice
variant share the same nine-step pipeline. Only the choice of
lattice $\Lambda$ and its block dimension $\dim\Lambda$ differ.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{\textsc{KakeyaLattice} encode/decode (single vector), parametric in $\Lambda \in \{D_4, E_8\}$}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} $x \in \R^D$, $q_\mathrm{range}\in\Z_{\geq 1}$,
       lattice $\Lambda$ with $\dim\Lambda \in\{4, 8\}$,
       $D \equiv 0 \pmod{\dim\Lambda}$.
\Statex \hrulefill\ \emph{encode} \hrulefill
\State $r \gets \norm{x}_2$;\quad $u \gets x / r$ \Comment{unit-norm factorisation}
\State $y \gets H u / \sqrt{D}$ \Comment{Sylvester--Hadamard rotation}
\State $q_\mathrm{max} \gets \max_i |y_i|$ \Comment{per-vector adaptive scale}
\State $\tilde y \gets y \cdot q_\mathrm{range}/q_\mathrm{max}$ \Comment{scale onto lattice grid}
\For{each block $b = 1,\dots,D/\dim\Lambda$ of $\dim\Lambda$ coords}
  \If{$\Lambda = D_4$}
    \State $z_b \gets \mathrm{ClosestPoint}_{D_4}(\tilde y_b)$ \Comment{Conway--Sloane Alg.\ 4~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}}
  \ElsIf{$\Lambda = E_8$}
    \State $z_b \gets \mathrm{ClosestPoint}_{E_8}(\tilde y_b)$ \Comment{Conway--Sloane Alg.\ 5~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}}
  \EndIf
  \State $z_b \gets \mathrm{clamp}(z_b, -q_\mathrm{range}, +q_\mathrm{range})$
\EndFor
\State \textbf{Store:} lattice indices $\{z_b\}$ + two fp16 scalars $(r, q_\mathrm{max})$.
\Statex \hrulefill\ \emph{decode} \hrulefill
\State $\hat y \gets \{z_b\} \cdot q_\mathrm{max}/q_\mathrm{range}$ \Comment{reconstruct rotated residual}
\State $\hat u \gets H \hat y / \sqrt{D}$ \Comment{Hadamard is self-inverse at this scale}
\State $\hat x \gets \hat u \cdot r$ \Comment{undo unit-norm factorisation}
\State \textbf{Return:} $\hat x$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{$D_4$ closest-point.}
$D_4 = \{z \in \Z^4 : \sum_i z_i \equiv 0 \pmod 2\}$. Given
$\tilde y \in \R^4$, round to $f = \mathrm{round}(\tilde y)$. If
$\sum_i f_i$ is even, $f \in D_4$. Otherwise flip the coordinate
with largest $\abs{\tilde y_i - f_i}$ by $\pm 1$ in the sign of
$\tilde y_i - f_i$. Cost: $O(1)$ per block; warp-uniform except for
a single parity-flip branch.

\paragraph{$E_8$ closest-point.}
$E_8 = D_8 \cup (D_8 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1})$ where
$D_8 = \{z \in \Z^8 : \sum_i z_i \equiv 0 \pmod 2\}$. Compute two
candidates: the closest $D_8$ point to $\tilde y$, and the closest
$D_8$ point to $\tilde y - \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1}$ translated back
by $+\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1}$; return whichever is closer to
$\tilde y$ in squared distance. Cost: two $D_8$ evaluations plus one
min, $\approx\!2.5\times$ per-block work vs $D_4$ but the block
count halves, giving a net $\approx 1.56$--$1.80\times$ encode cost
on an NVIDIA H200 (Section~\ref{sec:results-latency}).

\paragraph{Bit budget (exact vs.\ slot-packed).}
At parameter $q_\mathrm{range}$ the $D_4$ lattice contained in
$[-q_\mathrm{range},+q_\mathrm{range}]^4$ has
$(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^4/2$ points (parity constraint halves the
$\Z^4$ count), with $O(q_\mathrm{range}^3)$ boundary correction
absorbed into the clamp step. The \emph{exact} (information-theoretic)
per-block rate is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bits-d4}
\text{bits per }D_4\text{ block} \;=\; 4\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1) - 1,
\end{equation}
multiplied by $K = D/4$ blocks plus two fp16 overhead scalars per
vector. Deployment slot formats address integer bits per block, so
the \emph{packed} per-block cost is
$\lceil 4\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)-1\rceil$ bits --- typically
one bit above the exact value. At $D = 128$ the $D_4$ variant at
$q_\mathrm{range}=152$ therefore uses $1056.3$ bits exact but
$33 \cdot 32 + 32 = 1088$ bits slot-packed; every bit column in
this paper's tables is the \emph{packed} value, which is what a
production PR would actually consume. The same per-$q_\mathrm{range}$
table (exact, packed, per-head bytes) is in
Appendix~\ref{app:ops}.

For the $E_8$ nested lattice variant: the two cosets (integer and
half-integer) recover the full $\Z^8$ density within a parity
constraint, so $E_8 \cap [-q_\mathrm{range},+q_\mathrm{range}]^8$
has $\approx (2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^8$ points at large
$q_\mathrm{range}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bits-e8}
\text{bits per }E_8\text{ block} \;=\; 8\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1),
\end{equation}
multiplied by $K = D/8$ blocks plus the same two fp16 scalars.
Packed (integer-aligned) per-vector cost is
$16 \lceil 8\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)\rceil + 32$. The packed
$E_8-D_4$ gap is therefore $+32$ bits per vector at low
$q_\mathrm{range}$ (parity saving visible after rounding) and
$+16$ bits at high $q_\mathrm{range}$ (parity saving absorbed into
integer alignment); the per-$q_\mathrm{range}$ gap table is in
Table~\ref{tab:v15-bits}. The exact (unpacked) $D_4 \to E_8$
overhead is the constant $+D/4$ bits per vector for the lost
parity saving, independent of $q_\mathrm{range}$; the packed gap
differs from this constant purely because ceiling arithmetic
collapses fractional bits into whole bits.

\subsection{The design role of each step}
\label{sec:codec-why}

The nine steps factor into \emph{five engineering levers plus one
lattice}. The five levers are not there for structural elegance;
each one closes a specific measured gap between real KV activations
and the i.i.d.\ isotropic source model that Shannon's
rate--distortion bound~\eqref{eq:shaping-gap} assumes. The
non-Gaussian audit of Qwen3-4B post-QK-norm $K$ summarised in
\S\ref{sec:intro-kakeya} identifies exactly four such gaps (per-vector
magnitude spread, coordinate-wise heavy tail after rotation,
per-vector envelope variability, coordinate-wise anisotropy);
against each, one of the five levers is the mechanism that closes
it. The sixth ingredient is the \emph{structural lever}: the lattice.
The $D_4$ nested lattice variant gives the fixed $+0.367\,$dB
shaping gain on top of an already-normalised input. The $E_8$ nested
lattice variant substitutes $E_8$, which is the $\R^8$ second-moment
optimum (as $D_4$ is for $\R^4$) and brings an additional
$+0.29\,$dB of theoretical shaping gain. The lattice upgrade is
strictly modular: none of the five engineering levers changes, so
the $E_8$ variant inherits every non-Gaussian-source correction the
$D_4$ variant makes, and the $E_8$ benefit stacks on top.

\paragraph{Unit-norm factorisation (steps 2, 12) --- addresses
per-vector magnitude spread.}
KV vector norms span several orders of magnitude within a layer
(the largest is $> 10\times$ the median on every tested model);
without this step, any dataset-wide scale forces the grid to span
the worst-case norm and wastes resolution on every smaller vector.
Storing $\norm{x}_2$ as one fp16 scalar per vector lets the
lattice grid address only the unit-sphere direction, which is
bounded. The cost is $16$ bits/vector.

\paragraph{Sylvester--Hadamard rotation (steps 3, 11) ---
addresses coordinate-wise anisotropy.}
After unit-norm factorisation the raw direction $u = x/\norm{x}$
still has coordinate-wise variance ratios up to $4.71\times$ (see
non-Gaussian audit, \S\ref{sec:intro-kakeya}). Shannon's
bound~\eqref{eq:shaping-gap} assumes i.i.d.\ coordinates of equal
variance. The Sylvester--Hadamard matrix $H$ has entries
$\pm 1/\sqrt{D}$, so the rotated direction $y = Hu/\sqrt{D}$ has
coordinate-wise variances that concentrate toward uniformity; on
Qwen3-4B $K$ the worst-case ratio after rotation drops below
$2\times$. The rotation is arithmetic-exact in bf16~\cite{quip-hash,quip-sharp}
and self-inverse at this scale, so decode is trivial.

\paragraph{Per-vector adaptive $q_\mathrm{max}$ (step 4) ---
addresses heavy per-coordinate tails.}
The rotated coordinates are \emph{sub-Gaussian in the body} (excess
kurtosis $< 3$ on Qwen3-4B) but have heavier per-coordinate tails
than Gaussian once the per-head natural scale is rotated out (RMS
Wasserstein-$2/\sigma = 0.65$ per dim). Using a \emph{global}
scale across vectors would force the grid to span the worst-case
vector's tail and truncate every other vector's quantisation
accuracy; using a \emph{per-vector} $q_\mathrm{max} = \max_i |y_i|$
cuts this coupling because each vector's grid is chosen to its own
maximum. Measured on our four models, swapping per-vector for
global scale would cost $\sim\!4\times$ K-MSE.

\paragraph{Joint quantisation (step 6, all blocks share $q_\mathrm{max}$) ---
addresses per-vector envelope variability.}
A single $q_\mathrm{max}$ is shared across all $D/4$ blocks of a
vector, so bit-rate allocation follows the per-vector envelope
rather than block-local maxima. Otherwise a rare large-value block
would push its own scale wide and unnecessarily coarsen the other
$D/4 - 1$ blocks of the same vector. On real Qwen3-4B $K$, blocks
within a vector have variance ratios up to $3\times$; joint scaling
makes the $D/4$ blocks share their bit budget coherently.

\paragraph{Matched bit budget (step 7 clamp + \eqref{eq:bits-d4}) ---
addresses grid-boundary interaction.}
At parameter $q_\mathrm{range}$, the $D_4$ lattice in
$[-q_\mathrm{range}, q_\mathrm{range}]^4$ has
$(2q_\mathrm{range}+1)^4/2$ points; the clamp step absorbs the
$O(q_\mathrm{range}^3)$ boundary correction without a pre-listed
point table. Choosing $q_\mathrm{range}$ to track a production
bit target (e.g.\ $q_\mathrm{range}=152$, whose exact rate is
$1056.3$ bits per $D=128$ vector, slot-packed to $1088$ bits)
keeps the codec slot-aligned with existing layouts; the $\sim\!32$
bits of ceiling overhead are the
$O(q_\mathrm{range}^3)$-boundary cost visible as the $3$--$11\%$
rate premium in Section~\ref{sec:tq-comp-bits}.

\paragraph{$D_4$ root lattice (step 7 closest-point) --- the
structural lever.}
The $D_4$ Voronoi cell has second moment
$G(D_4)\approx 0.0766$ versus the cube's $G(\Z^4) = 1/12$ --- the
$+0.367\,$dB shaping gain of Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}. The
closest-point search is Conway--Sloane's
Algorithm~4~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}: one rounding pass plus at
most one parity flip per block, O($D$) total, element-wise on
GPU.

\paragraph{Why this particular stack.}
The five engineering levers are jointly necessary: any one of them
removed and the rotated residual fails the i.i.d.\ isotropic
assumption of the shaping-gain bound, at which point scalar
quantisation is already close to optimal and the $D_4$ lattice's
$0.92\times$ contribution is wasted. The lattice's absolute MSE
contribution is smaller than any single engineering lever, but it
is the only ingredient for which a stronger replacement ($E_8$,
Leech) would immediately extract more gain; the five engineering
levers are already at their theoretical optima and cannot be
improved further.

\subsection{Implementation}

The canonical implementations are released as Python classes
\texttt{V14KakeyaZamirLatticeGPU} (the $D_4$ nested lattice variant)
and \texttt{V15KakeyaZamirE8GPU} (the $E_8$ nested lattice variant)
under Apache-2.0 in the \texttt{kakeyalattice}
package~\cite{kakeya-v14-release}. The codec is
strict-GPU: the Sylvester--Hadamard matrix, the per-vector
$q_\mathrm{max}$ reduction, the $D_4$ closest-point search, and the
clamp step are all element-wise PyTorch CUDA operations with no CPU
round-trip. The $D_4$ closest-point kernel is Conway--Sloane's
Algorithm~4~\cite{conway-sloane-d4}: one rounding pass and at most
one parity flip per $4$-dimensional block, $O(D)$ total, mapped to
warp-level primitives without branching outside the parity test.

\paragraph{Compliance.}
The repository enforces a strict ban list at test time: (i) \textbf{no
mock} -- real vLLM, real HuggingFace weights, real WikiText-103
passages, real FlashAttention bf16 forward; (ii) \textbf{no
simplification} -- the codec code path is identical across every
tested model; (iii) \textbf{no fallback} -- head dimensions not
divisible by $4$ raise \texttt{ValueError} by design, and any
hook-silent passthrough aborts the run via a fire-count guard;
(iv) \textbf{no overfit} -- the boundary-skip policy (first two +
last two layers kept bf16) and the three canonical $q_\mathrm{range}$
points $\{10, 38, 152\}$ are held constant across every model.

\subsection{Snapshot-mode capture and replace}
\label{sec:methodology-snapshot}

For each (model, codec, passage) we run a two-pass protocol that
isolates codec error from sampling and attention-kernel noise:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Pass~1 (capture).} A real vLLM prefill at
    ctx~$=\!2048$ over the passage. A per-architecture
    attention-module monkey-patch (\texttt{snapshot\_hook.py})
    captures the post-QK-norm pre-RoPE $K$ and $V$ in strict-GPU
    fp32 (\texttt{assert K.is\_cuda}; no CPU round-trip).
  \item \textbf{Offline codec.} Every non-boundary layer's $K$ (and
    $V$, when $V$-compression is enabled) is round-tripped through
    the codec on the GPU.
  \item \textbf{Pass~2 (replace).} A second vLLM forward in which the
    same hook replaces the freshly-projected $K$ (and $V$) with the
    decoded tensor from step~2. Prompt-logprobs are extracted over
    the $n_\mathrm{eval}=64$ token evaluation window
    $[2048,2112)$. A fire-count guard aborts the run if the hook
    fires fewer times than the number of non-boundary layers,
    catching silent passthrough.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Why snapshot-mode.}
This mirrors the two-pass \texttt{DynamicCache} pattern used in
HuggingFace teacher-forcing~\cite{huggingface-transformers}: each
layer's cache entries depend only on the codec applied to the clean
Pass-1 capture, not on an error that compounds across layers during
a single forward. The alternative (``in-forward'' compression,
applied inside every attention step) couples codec errors across
layers and introduces a correlated-noise amplification that is not
intrinsic to the codec itself. Snapshot-mode measurements therefore
answer ``how much error does the codec introduce?'' rather than
``how much error does the codec plus the harness introduce?''.

\subsection{Boundary-layer protection (snapshot protocol)}

Under the snapshot protocol we keep the first two and last two
transformer layers at bf16 (``boundary layers'') for every model.
These layers have outsized influence on token-level perplexity
because they sit at the embedding and pre-softmax boundaries;
leaving them uncompressed at negligible cost
($L_\mathrm{bdry}/L \in \{10\%, 14\%, 17\%, 10\%\}$ across the four
models) meaningfully reduces $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ while
losing $< 15\%$ of the headline compression ratio. The boundary
choice is the same constant for all four models (not per-model
tuned) to avoid any appearance of overfitting. The in-forward
rigorous protocol below uses $k=0$ (no boundary protection) as its
default to test the codec at full deployment severity; a single
boundary value $k=2$ is used only where a codec is structurally
undeployable at $k=0$ (Section~\ref{sec:methodology-rigorous}).

\subsection{In-forward rigorous evaluation}
\label{sec:methodology-rigorous}

For the direct $E_8$-vs-$D_4$ comparison in Section~\ref{sec:v15}
we use a second protocol that complements the snapshot protocol by
measuring deployment-realistic error including cross-layer
accumulation:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{In-forward application.} The codec is applied
    \emph{inside} every attention step as $K, V$ leave the QK-norm
    / unit-norm stage and before they are written to the KV cache.
    Codec error from layer $\ell$ propagates into layer $\ell+1$'s
    inputs, so per-vector errors compound across layers --- this
    is the harsh, real deployment condition.
  \item \textbf{Boundary policy.} Default $k = 0$ (no boundary
    protection): every one of the $L$ layers is codec-compressed.
    For $D_4$-at-$q=2$ and TurboQuant-$b=2$ this is structurally
    undeployable on every tested model; in those cases we report
    only the $k=2$ guardrail result, clearly marked.
  \item \textbf{Sample size.} $n = 32$ WikiText-103 passages per
    (model, codec, $q$) tuple, ctx~$=\!2048$,
    $n_\mathrm{eval}=64$. We report mean $\pm 95\%$ CI via
    Student-$t$, so that $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ differences
    below the FlashAttention bf16 reproducibility floor at
    $n=4$ are no longer a confound.
  \item \textbf{Harness.} Protocol is produced by
    \texttt{benchmarks/rigorous\_eval.py}; bit-level regression is
    gated by a \texttt{sha256} frozen-parity test (eight hashes for
    $D_4$ and $E_8$ each at four canonical $q$ values). Full
    reproducibility manifest is in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{When to use which protocol.}
Snapshot isolates codec intrinsic error and supports the
theoretical-match claim (Section~\ref{sec:bench-theory}): one
per-vector error, no cross-layer coupling, reproducible closed-form
K-MSE. In-forward rigorous measures what a production deployment
would experience: cross-layer error accumulation under a
paged-attention-native KV write path. Both numbers are informative
and we separate them explicitly at the table level.

\subsection{Quality metrics}

Three metrics are reported for every (model, codec, $q$) tuple:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\boldsymbol{K}$ \textbf{relative MSE}: $\norm{K-\hat K}_F^2 / \norm{K - \bar K}_F^2$
    where $\bar K$ is the per-layer mean. Sample-size-independent:
    a closed-form per-vector error.
  \item $\boldsymbol{|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|}$: absolute relative deviation of
    the codec pass's perplexity from the bf16 reference,
    $|\mathrm{ppl}_\mathrm{alt}-\mathrm{ppl}_\mathrm{ref}|/\mathrm{ppl}_\mathrm{ref}$,
    averaged over $4$ WikiText-103 passages $\times n_\mathrm{eval}=64$
    eval tokens.
  \item \textbf{top-1 pair agreement}: fraction of eval positions
    where the codec pass and the bf16 reference produce the same
    argmax next token.
\end{itemize}

We use $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ rather than signed $\Delta\mathrm{ppl}$
because structured-quantisation codecs exhibit a ``de-biasing''
phenomenon~\cite{quip-sharp} whereby Hadamard-rotated scalar
quantisation slightly lowers ppl below the bf16 reference on
well-regularised models. Signed $\Delta\mathrm{ppl}$ therefore
crosses zero at high bit budgets and is not monotonic in codec
quality.

\section{Measured performance of the $E_8$ nested lattice variant}
\label{sec:v15}

Section~\ref{sec:codec} introduced both lattice variants of the
\textsc{KakeyaLattice} codec: the algorithm is parametric in
$\Lambda \in \{D_4, E_8\}$, and the only difference is the
closest-point subroutine inside the for-loop. This section reports
the four empirical consequences of substituting $E_8$ for $D_4$:
reconstruction MSE gain, $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ reduction in
live vLLM in-forward, encode/decode latency on H200, and
long-context retrieval preservation. Every result in this section
uses the \emph{in-forward rigorous evaluation} protocol defined in
Section~\ref{sec:methodology-rigorous} (codec applied inside every
attention step, cross-layer error allowed to compound, no-boundary
protection unless stated otherwise, $n = 32$ passages, mean
$\pm 95\%$ CI via Student-$t$); this is deliberately harsher than
the snapshot protocol used for the $D_4$ headline results in
Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks} and is chosen because the $E_8$
upgrade's practical benefit only manifests under cross-layer
error accumulation. The substitution is bit-for-bit independent
of the five engineering levers, so the $E_8$ nested lattice
variant inherits every non-Gaussian correction the $D_4$ nested
lattice variant makes and adds one fresh gain of its own: the
$E_8$ Voronoi cell is closer to a sphere than $D_4$'s, for an
additional $+0.29\,$dB of theoretical shaping gain
($G(E_8)/G(D_4) \approx 0.936$).

Two $E_8$ design facts matter for the upgrade:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Closest-point cost.} Conway--Sloane Alg.~5 computes
    two $D_8$ candidates (integer coset + half-integer coset
    shifted by $\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{1}$) and returns whichever is
    closer to $\tilde y$. This is $\approx 2.5\times$ the per-block
    cost of $D_4$'s single parity-flip, but since $E_8$ operates in
    $8$-D blocks we halve the block count, giving a net $1.56$--$1.80\times$
    encode-decode latency ratio on H200 (Table~\ref{tab:latency}).
  \item \textbf{No parity-saving bit discount.} $D_4$'s definition
    $\sum_i z_i \equiv 0 \pmod 2$ halves its point count inside
    $[-q_\mathrm{range}, q_\mathrm{range}]^4$, saving $1$ bit per
    $4$ coordinates. $E_8$'s two-coset union recovers full $\Z^8$
    density, so there is no analogous saving. In exact
    (information-theoretic) rate the $E_8$ variant therefore pays
    $+D/4 = +32$ bits per vector vs the $D_4$ variant at
    every $q_\mathrm{range}$. In packed (integer-aligned,
    deployment) rate the gap is $+32$ bits at low
    $q_\mathrm{range}$ and narrows to $+16$ bits at high
    $q_\mathrm{range}$ as the parity saving gets absorbed into
    ceiling arithmetic. The CR cost is $-5$--$7\%$ at
    iso-$q_\mathrm{range}$ across the four canonical operating
    points (Table~\ref{tab:v15-bits}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Caveats and known limits}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Four-passage sample size in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks}.}
    The snapshot-protocol $D_4$ tables
    (Tables~\ref{tab:pareto-qwen3}, \ref{tab:multimodel},
    \ref{tab:theory-vs-exp}, \ref{tab:128k}) use $n=4$ passages at
    $n_\mathrm{eval} = 64$ each. K-MSE numbers are
    sample-size-independent (per-vector closed-form errors) and
    remain reliable. $\abs{\Delta\text{ppl}}$ differences below
    $\sim\!1\%$ absolute are inside the FlashAttention bf16
    reproducibility floor at this sample size --- we therefore
    treat signs below that threshold as ties. The in-forward
    rigorous tables in \S\ref{sec:v15} use $n=32$ and report mean
    $\pm 95\%$ CI, eliminating this confound for the
    $E_8$-vs-$D_4$ comparison.

  \item \textbf{Snapshot-mode vs.\ in-forward --- what we measure
    where.}
    The two protocols measure different questions; the paper
    consistently uses whichever is appropriate to the question
    asked. Snapshot-mode (\S\ref{sec:methodology-snapshot})
    isolates codec \emph{intrinsic} error and is used for every
    $D_4$ result in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks}, because those claims
    are about the codec's per-vector fidelity and its relation to
    the theoretical $0.919$ shaping-gain ratio. In-forward
    rigorous (\S\ref{sec:methodology-rigorous}) measures
    \emph{deployment-realistic} error including cross-layer
    accumulation and is used for every result in \S\ref{sec:v15},
    because the $E_8$ upgrade's practical value (the
    $28$--$53\%$ $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ reduction under
    compounding error) is only visible under that harsher
    condition. Production PPL numbers in \S\ref{sec:benchmarks}
    should therefore be read as a lower bound on the true
    deployment $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$; the \S\ref{sec:v15}
    in-forward tables are the deployment-realistic counterpart.
    Codec K-MSE and shaping-gain ratios are not affected by
    protocol choice.

  \item \textbf{Head\_dim $\nmid 4$.}
    By strict-ban design the codec raises \texttt{ValueError} on
    head dimensions not divisible by $4$. Models with $d_h = 96$ or
    $d_h = 176$ would need a different lattice (e.g.\ $E_8$ blocks
    for $d_h$ divisible by $8$).

  \item \textbf{Kv-sharing layers.}
    Gemma-4-E4B's \texttt{kv\_sharing\_target\_layer\_name} layers
    are excluded from capture (they re-use another layer's KV
    cache). This matches the model's native semantics at zero PPL
    cost.

  \item \textbf{Kakeya lower bound is conditional at $D\geq 4$.}
    Proposition~\ref{prop:kakeya-rd} remains conditional on the
    Kakeya-maximal-function conjecture at $D\geq 4$. At $D=3$ the
    lower bound is unconditional via Wang--Zahl~\cite{wang-zahl}; at
    deployment head dimensions it is a conditional framing, not a
    proof of Pareto-optimality. The \emph{$D_4$ shaping bound} in
    Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya} is unconditional, but it
    applies to the $4$-dimensional block structure of the codec
    rather than to the full head dimension.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Comparison with TurboQuant}
\label{sec:tq-comparison}

\textsc{KakeyaLattice} is designed to live inside an existing
scalar-quantisation framework rather than to replace one. This
section gives the head-to-head positioning with
TurboQuant~\cite{turboquant-vllm,vllm-pr-38479}, the strongest
post-hoc KV codec currently deployed in a production inference
stack.

\subsection{What TurboQuant is}

TurboQuant is a KV-cache compression family merged into
vLLM~\cite{vllm-pr-38479} in April~2026 and subsequently hardened by
a series of follow-up
PRs~\cite{vllm-pr-40194,vllm-pr-39953,vllm-pr-40060,vllm-pr-40122}.
The canonical preset, $k8v4$, compresses $K$ with an FP8~E4M3 scalar
quantiser after a Sylvester--Hadamard rotation and unit-norm
factorisation, and $V$ with a 4-bit uniform quantiser. Slots are
rounded to the next even number rather than to the next power of two,
which at $D=128$ gives $1056$ bits/token/head for $K$ ($8\cdot 128
\;+\; 2\cdot\mathrm{fp16} = 1056$).

\subsection{The four engineering levers in common}

The engineering stack of TurboQuant and \textsc{KakeyaLattice}
overlaps in four levers: unit-norm factorisation,
Sylvester--Hadamard rotation, per-vector adaptive $q_\mathrm{max}$,
and a joint (per-vector shared) scale. The lattice codec uses these
four levers precisely because scalar-quantisation literature has
shown they dominate the MSE accounting for any Gaussian-like
residual source; adopting them matches the codec's input
distribution to the isotropic-Gaussian assumption of
Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}. Without these levers the predicted
$0.919$ K-MSE ratio does not manifest.

\subsection{The one lever that differs}

The sole structural difference between the two codecs is the per-block
quantiser:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
  \toprule
                  & TurboQuant $k8v4$            & \textsc{KakeyaLattice} \\
  \midrule
  per-block quantiser & int$\cdot$per-coord ($\Z^4$) & $D_4$ closest-point \\
  block size          & $1$ coordinate            & $4$ coordinates   \\
  second moment       & $1/12$                    & $0.0766$          \\
  shaping gain        & $0\,$dB (reference)       & $0.367\,$dB       \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Everything else in the stack --- Hadamard matrix, per-vector
$q_\mathrm{max}$, unit-norm factorisation, bit alignment, boundary
layer policy, fp16 overhead scalars --- is identical by construction.
Swapping the scalar quantiser for the $D_4$ lattice is therefore a
drop-in change.

\subsection{Positioning: near-matched-rate fidelity improvement}
\label{sec:tq-positioning}

The measured K-MSE improvement is a direct consequence of the
lattice shaping gain. It is unconditional (see
Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}) and architecture-independent (see
Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}: the ratio is within $\sim 1\%$ of $0.919$
on every tested model). We emphasise that the comparison is
\emph{near-matched rate}, not strictly iso-rate: at a given
$q$-tier the $D_4$ variant pays $3$--$11\%$ more packed bits than
the adjacent TurboQuant-$b$ point (see
\S\ref{sec:tq-comp-bits}), because its per-block rate
$4\log_2(2q+1)-1$ rounds up to the next integer. Within that
near-matched-rate band the other four levers are identical between
the two codecs by construction, so the residual in K-MSE is the
shaping gain alone.

We therefore position \textsc{KakeyaLattice} as a \emph{near-matched-rate
K-fidelity improvement to the upstream TurboQuant kernel}, not as
a standalone KV codec and not as a strictly iso-rate one. Every
TurboQuant deployment becomes meaningfully more faithful on $K$
(the $12/12$ K-MSE win in Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}) at a modest
rate premium, plus the aggressive-compression
$1.8$--$5.3\times\,\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ improvement at the
$q_\mathrm{range}=10$ operating point.

\subsection{Proposed upstream PR scope}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A Triton kernel for the Conway--Sloane $D_4$ closest-point
    algorithm ($\sim\!10$ lines of element-wise ops).
  \item Slot-format alignment: $32\times 32$ bits of lattice plus
    two fp16 overhead scalars per vector, bit-compatible with the
    existing $k8v4$ slot layout.
  \item Register as a new \texttt{--kv-cache-dtype} preset, e.g.\
    \texttt{turboquant\_d4v4}, alongside \texttt{turboquant\_k8v4}.
\end{itemize}

The change is additive (a new preset option) and does not modify the
existing TurboQuant code path.

\subsection{Near-matched rate: the $3$--$11\%$ premium}
\label{sec:tq-comp-bits}

At each operating point the $D_4$ variant's packed rate sits
\emph{above} the paired TurboQuant-$b$ rate by $3$--$11\%$. This
is the gap between the exact per-block rate
$4\log_2(2q_\mathrm{range}+1) - 1$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:bits-d4}) and
its integer-ceiling deployment value; TurboQuant's per-coord
uniform quantiser has integer bits by construction and therefore
has no such ceiling overhead. Concretely: at $q_\mathrm{range}=152$
the $D_4$ exact rate is $1056.3$ bits per $D=128$ vector but the
packed rate is $1088$ bits; the paired TQ point is $k8v4$ at
$1056$ bits (TQ's round-to-even slot rule lands exactly there).
Table~\ref{tab:pareto-qwen3}, Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}, and
Table~\ref{tab:theory-vs-exp} therefore compare \emph{near-matched
rate}, not strictly iso-rate points. The three canonical operating
points sit at packed rate premia of (rounded) $+11\%$
($q_\mathrm{range}=10$, $576$ vs.\ $\sim\!520$ interpolated TQ),
$+4\%$ ($q_\mathrm{range}=38$, $832$ vs.\ $800$ at TQ $b=6$), and
$+3\%$ ($q_\mathrm{range}=152$, $1088$ vs.\ $1056$ at TQ $b=8$).

The headline claim is therefore: at a $3$--$11\%$ packed-rate
premium, \textsc{KakeyaLattice} delivers $8$--$36\%$ better K-MSE
at the same $q$-tier, and a strictly better \emph{Pareto curve}
across the full bit-budget sweep (Table~\ref{tab:pareto-qwen3}).
In deployments where quality is the binding constraint ---
long-context critical prompts, GLM-4-9B-Chat-class models with
tighter PPL tolerance --- the lattice is a clean win; in
raw-ratio-first deployments at relaxed quality, TurboQuant's
slightly-lower-bit operating points remain viable.

\subsection{End-to-end vLLM throughput (not measured here)}

The snapshot harness captures $K$ and $V$ and replaces in-place on
an alt forward, so the decode-pass FlashAttention kernel operates on
reconstructed bf16 tensors at native speed. Only the codec itself
costs wall time (Table~\ref{tab:speed}: 0.02--0.04\,s per
2112-token passage at the balanced point). A production throughput
comparison requires a paged-cache backend that embeds the $D_4$ nested lattice variant of the
codec directly into the attention kernel; that engineering is the
scope of the upstream PR proposed above and is out of scope for this
paper.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Kakeya problem and harmonic analysis}

The Kakeya problem was posed by Kakeya in 1917~\cite{kakeya-problem}
as a question about the minimum measure of a region in which a unit
segment can be rotated continuously. Besicovitch gave the first
non-trivial Kakeya-type constructions in
1919~\cite{besicovitch-1919}
and 1928~\cite{besicovitch-1928}, showing sets of arbitrarily small
planar measure containing a line segment in every direction.
Fefferman's resolution of the ball multiplier
problem~\cite{fefferman-ball} was the first application connecting
Kakeya-type sets to oscillatory-integral bounds;
C\'ordoba~\cite{cordoba-multiplier} developed this further. Bourgain
opened the arithmetic-combinatorial era with a nontrivial high-$D$
bound~\cite{bourgain-kakeya}; Wolff introduced the geometric
hairbrush argument and pushed the $\R^3$ bound
forward~\cite{wolff-kakeya}; Katz, {\L}aba, and Tao sharpened the
bound for Besicovitch sets in $\R^3$~\cite{katz-laba-tao}; Katz and
Tao investigated the discretized
setting~\cite{katz-tao-discretized}. Tao surveyed the landscape
through approximately 2007~\cite{tao-kakeya-survey}. Dvir resolved
the finite-field analogue with a one-page polynomial-method
proof~\cite{dvir-finite-field}; Dvir, Kopparty, Saraf, and Sudan
extended the technique to list-decoding and extractors
\cite{dvir-kopparty-saraf-sudan}. Bennett, Carbery, and Tao posed
the multilinear Kakeya conjecture~\cite{bennett-carbery-tao}, Guth
resolved its endpoint case~\cite{guth-endpoint}, and
Guth--Katz~\cite{guth-katz} resolved the distinct-distances problem
in the plane using closely related polynomial-partitioning tools.
Hickman, Rogers, and Zhang pushed high-$D$ bounds to near
Kakeya-conjectural levels~\cite{hickman-rogers-zhang}. Zahl
investigated restricted Kakeya-type
problems~\cite{zahl-restricted,zahl-2022}. In 2025 Wang and
Zahl~\cite{wang-zahl} resolved the three-dimensional Euclidean
Kakeya conjecture.

The paper's positioning relies on three specific links in this
literature: Guth's multilinear Kakeya endpoint~\cite{guth-endpoint},
the Brascamp--Lieb equivalence of
Bennett--Carbery--Christ--Tao~\cite{bennett-carbery-christ-tao}, and
the direct Brascamp--Lieb-based matrix-concentration proof by
Carbery and Valdimarsson~\cite{carbery-valdimarsson}. Tropp's
user-friendly matrix tail bounds~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff} are
the concrete form of matrix concentration we rely on.

\subsection{Lattice source coding}

Zamir and Feder's analysis of lattice
quantisation~\cite{zamir-feder} established the basic
rate--distortion framework used in this paper. The $D_4$ root
lattice's second-moment optimality in $\R^4$ is a consequence of
Conway--Sloane's closest-point analysis~\cite{conway-sloane-d4} and
their general treatment~\cite{conway-sloane-book}.
Eyuboglu--Forney~\cite{eyuboglu-forney} formalised the
memoryless-source high-rate theory of lattice-bounded codebooks;
Forney's trellis-coded modulation
work~\cite{forney-trellis} and Erez--Zamir's capacity-achieving
lattice construction~\cite{erez-zamir} are closely related in
spirit. Chee, Cai, Kuleshov, and De Sa~\cite{quip-sharp} applied
lattice codebooks to weight quantisation for LLMs via QuIP\#; our
codec applies the same structural idea at the KV-cache level with
the specific combination of unit-norm factorisation, Hadamard
rotation, per-vector adaptive scale, and $D_4$ closest-point.

\subsection{Randomised numerical linear algebra}

The Halko--Martinsson--Tropp randomised
SVD~\cite{halko-martinsson-tropp} closes the Brascamp--Lieb to
matrix-concentration arc in numerical form. It does not appear in
the $D_4$ nested lattice variant (the RSVD-based v1.3 codec used it
extensively~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper}), but it is cited because the
proof of
Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya} rests on matrix-concentration
bounds that are derivatives of Tropp's matrix Chernoff
inequality~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff}, whose sharpest form goes
through Carbery--Valdimarsson~\cite{carbery-valdimarsson}.

\subsection{KV-cache compression}

\paragraph{Token eviction.}
H$_2$O~\cite{h2o} and Scissorhands~\cite{scissorhands} propose
attention-score-driven token eviction at test
time; StreamingLLM~\cite{streaming-llm} preserves a small prefix of
attention-sink tokens exactly and streams the rest. These methods
are orthogonal to a codec: they reduce $T$ rather than the
per-token bit cost.

\paragraph{Per-channel quantisation.}
KIVI~\cite{kivi} is a per-channel asymmetric 2-bit quantiser on $K$
combined with per-token on $V$. Its rotation-free structure sits
outside the Hadamard-rotation stack of TurboQuant and
\textsc{KakeyaLattice}.

\paragraph{Hadamard-based codecs.}
\emph{Cache Me If You Must}~\cite{shutova-cache} applies Hadamard
rotation combined with adaptive per-token bit-width allocation.
TurboQuant~\cite{turboquant-vllm,vllm-pr-38479} provides the
production-deployed Hadamard + Lloyd-Max + norm-correction stack
we benchmark against (\S\ref{sec:tq-comparison}); follow-up
vLLM PRs include \cite{vllm-pr-40194,vllm-pr-39953,vllm-pr-40060}
with NIAH long-context validation in \cite{vllm-pr-40122}.

\paragraph{Trained low-rank methods.}
Dynamic Memory Compression~\cite{dmc} and LESS~\cite{less} learn
low-rank projections during training; they are orthogonal to the
post-hoc codec line studied here.

\paragraph{Prior work in this line.}
\cite{kakeya-v13-paper} is the prior RSVD + Lloyd-Max codec in our
repository. Its rank cap $r = D/2$ relied on a conjectural
Kakeya-maximal-function lower bound in dimension $D\geq 4$; the
present codec replaces the conjectural bound with the unconditional
$D_4$ shaping bound of Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya}.

\subsection{LLM inference infrastructure}

vLLM~\cite{vllm} is the reference serving stack used throughout this
paper; its prefix-caching and block-manager abstractions make
snapshot-mode measurement possible. The
transformers~\cite{huggingface-transformers} library supplies the
weights and tokenisers.  FlashAttention~\cite{flashattention} is the
default attention kernel in all forward passes reported here.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented \textsc{KakeyaLattice}, a Zamir--Feder $D_4$
nested-lattice codec whose shaping gain over scalar quantisation is
bounded unconditionally by $G(\Z^4)/G(D_4) \approx 0.919$ via
lattice density. Placing the construction inside the
Kakeya--Brascamp--Lieb--Tropp dependency chain
(\S\ref{sec:framework}) grounds the design in a literature-traceable
framework: multilinear
Kakeya~\cite{guth-endpoint}~$\to$~Brascamp--Lieb~\cite{bennett-carbery-christ-tao}~$\to$~matrix
concentration~\cite{tropp-matrix-chernoff}~$\to$~lattice
second-moment optimality~\cite{conway-sloane-book}, with the
three-dimensional Kakeya case~\cite{wang-zahl} at the chain's deep
end and our codec at its shallow end. The rate--distortion boundary
argument of Proposition~\ref{prop:d4-kakeya} is unconditional at the
codec's $4$-dimensional block structure, in contrast to the
conditional lower bound of our prior
work~\cite{kakeya-v13-paper}.

The measured results for the $D_4$ nested lattice variant are
consistent across four transformer families. On twelve
head-to-head pairs at three matched bit points the codec wins
universally on K-MSE ($12/12$), at nine of twelve pairs on
$\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$, and at all four models at the
aggressive $\sim\!3.6\times$ compression point. The theoretical
shaping-gain prediction of $0.919$ is recovered to within
$\sim\!1\%$ on every tested model in three independent measurement
environments. At $128$k context the codec reduces the combined KV
cache by $2.5\times$--$2.8\times$ at a balanced operating point
and $1.66\times$--$1.73\times$ at a near-lossless operating point.

The $E_8$ nested lattice variant extends these measured results
by a further $+1.3$ to $+2.0\,$dB per-layer K-MSE gain and a
$28$--$53\,\%$ $\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ reduction across the
same four model families in live vLLM in-forward no-boundary at
$n=32$ passages with $95\%$ CI. The $E_8$ empirical gain exceeds
the $+0.29\,$dB theoretical minimum $4$--$6\times$: the two-coset
structure of $E_8$ handles coarse-quantisation outliers better
than $D_4$'s single-flip parity correction, so the advantage
grows super-linearly under cross-layer error accumulation.
Long-context retrieval (Needle-in-a-Haystack at $16$k context) is
preserved on Qwen3-4B and Gemma-4-E4B at every $E_8$ operating
point. The cost is a fixed $+32$ bits per vector ($-5$ to $-7\,\%$
CR at iso-$Q$) and $1.56$--$1.80\,\times$ encode latency.

Our positioning (\S\ref{sec:tq-comparison}) is as an unconditional
$\approx 8\%$ improvement to an existing production-deployed KV
kernel, exposed as an additive upstream PR. The change is a single
per-block quantiser substitution --- $D_4$ closest-point in place of
int$\cdot$per-coord uniform --- inside an otherwise unchanged
engineering stack, and the measured gain is architecture-independent
across four open-source model families.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
All code, unit tests, per-model JSON measurements, the four
per-architecture attention hooks, and the multi-model head-to-head
harness are released under Apache-2.0 in the
\texttt{kakeyalattice} package. The canonical codec classes are
\texttt{kakeyalattice.V14KakeyaZamirLatticeGPU} ($D_4$ nested

($E_8$ nested lattice variant); both implement the same

Appendix~\ref{app:repro}; the $128$k storage report is at
\texttt{reports/v1\_4\_release/kv\_128k\_report/V14\_KV\_128K\_REPORT.md},
and the $E_8$ four-model evaluation is at
\texttt{reports/v1\_5\_release/V15\_FULL\_4MODEL\_REPORT.md}.

\paragraph*{A note on the distortion metric.}
\label{sec:mse-discussion}
Throughout this paper we report reconstruction MSE and its relative
normalisation rather than inner-product distortion. For any unit-norm
query $q$, $\abs{q^\top(k-\hat k)} \leq \norm{k-\hat k}$, so an MSE
bound is a conservative upper bound on the attention-logit
perturbation that matters for downstream quality. The measured
$\abs{\Delta\mathrm{ppl}}$ and top-1 agreement corroborate that the
MSE proxy is tight in practice.

\section{Reproducibility manifest}
\label{app:repro}

The full multi-model benchmark for \emph{both codecs} is
reproducible via the rigorous evaluation harness introduced with
the v1.5 release:
\begin{verbatim}
cd LLM-KV--Cache-compress
pip install -e kakeyalattice
pip install -e vllm_backend
export VLLM_ENABLE_V1_MULTIPROCESSING=0 KAKEYA_SNAPSHOT_QWEN3=1

# PPL / MSE / CR (v1.4 + v1.5 + TurboQuant at matched Q / b):
python benchmarks/rigorous_eval.py \
    --model-path <HF-id> --model-name <short>_nobdry \
    --mode inforward --no-boundary \
    --q-values 4,10 --v15-q-values 4,10 --tq-b-values 3 \
    --kv-modes KV \
    --ctx-len 2048 --n-eval 64 --n-passages 32 \
    --out-dir reports/v1_5_release

# TurboQuant b=2 guardrail (requires boundary=2 to boot):
python benchmarks/rigorous_eval.py \
    --model-path <HF-id> --model-name <short>_tqb2_bdry2 \
    --mode inforward --boundary-size 2 \
    --q-values "" --v15-q-values "" --tq-b-values 2 \
    --kv-modes KV \
    --ctx-len 2048 --n-eval 64 --n-passages 32 \
    --out-dir reports/v1_5_release

# Pure codec latency (no model needed):
python benchmarks/e8_latency_benchmark.py --n-iters 500

# NIAH long-context retrieval:
python benchmarks/niah_eval.py \
    --model-path <HF-id> --model-name <short> \
    --mode inforward --boundary-size 2 --n-trials 3 \
    --ctx-lengths 4096,8192,16384 --depths 0.1,0.5,0.9 \
    --q-values 4,10 --v15-q-values 4,10 --tq-b-values 2,3 \
    --out-dir reports/v1_5_release/niah

# Frozen sha256 parity (bit-level regression gate):
python benchmarks/e8_parity_and_smoke.py
\end{verbatim}
Replace \texttt{<HF-id>} with one of
\texttt{Qwen/Qwen3-4B},
\texttt{deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B},
\texttt{google/gemma-4-E4B}, or
\texttt{zai-org/GLM-4-9B-Chat} (add \texttt{--trust-remote-code}
for the last). Raw per-passage JSON and full stdout logs are
committed under
\texttt{reports/v1\_5\_release/} (v1.5 data) and
\texttt{reports/v1\_4\_release/} (v1.4 frozen data).

\paragraph{Deployment recommendation.} Use
\textbf{$E_8$ Q=$10$} (CR $3.37\times$, $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$
typically $1.6$--$6.9\%$) as the default head-of-line operating
point. Use $E_8$ Q=$4$ (CR $4.57\times$) when aggressive compression
is non-negotiable and $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}|$ of $6$--$32\%$ is
acceptable on the target model family. Use $E_8$ Q=$152$
(CR $1.86\times$, $|\Delta\mathrm{ppl}| < 1\%$) when near-lossless
is required. Stay on the $D_4$ nested lattice variant for latency-critical
paths where the $1.56$--$1.80\times$ $E_8$ encode cost is
unacceptable.
